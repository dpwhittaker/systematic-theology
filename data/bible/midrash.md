# The Interpretive Tradition

Midrash is the Jewish practice of interpreting Torah creatively, filling gaps in the narrative, exploring contradictions, asking "what if?" and "why?" It's not dispassionate scholarship—it's passionate engagement.

## What Midrash Does

**Fills narrative gaps:**
Scripture often leaves things unsaid. Midrash fills these silences with imaginative interpretation.

**Example - Abraham and the Idols:**
Genesis doesn't tell us how Abraham came to know the one God. He just appears called by God in Genesis 12.

**Midrash (Genesis Rabbah 38:13):** Young Abraham worked in his father Terah's idol shop. One day, he smashed all the idols except the largest, then placed a stick in the big idol's hand. When Terah asked what happened, Abraham said "The largest idol got angry and smashed the others." Terah replied, "Idols can't do that!" Abraham responded, "Then why do you worship them?"

Is this "what really happened"? The text doesn't say. But midrash explores: How did Abraham come to reject idolatry? What might have led him to the one God?

**Resolves contradictions:**
When the text seems to contradict itself, midrash doesn't smooth it over—it explores both sides.

**Explores theological problems:**
Why does God test Abraham? How could a good God command child sacrifice? Midrash doesn't give pat answers—it wrestles.

**Applies to new situations:**
How does this ancient text speak to us now? Midrash bridges past and present.

## Types of Midrash

**Midrash Halakha** - Legal interpretation
How do we apply Torah's commands? What does "remember the Sabbath" mean in practice?

**Midrash Aggadah** - Narrative interpretation
Story, theology, ethics. More creative, less binding. This is where the imaginative gap-filling happens.

## The Talmudic Method

The **Talmud** (completed ~500-600 AD) is the written record of centuries of rabbinic debate about Torah.

**Structure:**
- **Mishnah** (core text, ~200 AD): Brief legal rulings and teachings
- **Gemara** (commentary, ~500-600 AD): Rabbis arguing about the Mishnah

**What's stunning:** The Talmud preserves *both sides* of arguments. Minority opinions are recorded alongside majority rulings. Contradictions are left unresolved.

**Example format:**
*Rabbi Akiva says:* [Opinion A]
*Rabbi Ishmael says:* [Opinion B, contradicting A]
*The sages taught:* [Opinion C, different from both]

And often, no resolution. Just the record of the debate.

## *Eilu v'eilu* - These and These

**Talmud, Eruvin 13b:**

"For three years, the House of Hillel and the House of Shammai disputed. These said, 'The law follows our opinion,' and these said, 'The law follows our opinion.' A heavenly voice proclaimed: '*Eilu v'eilu divrei Elohim chaim*'—These and these are the words of the living God. But the law follows the House of Hillel."

Read that again: **Both sides are "the words of the living God"**—even though they contradict each other.

How is this possible?

**The Hebrew answer:** Truth is not univocal. Reality—especially divine reality—is richer than human logic. Two contradictory statements can both be true from different perspectives.

God's word is living, layered, multifaceted. It has seventy faces. The text holds more than one meaning.

**The Greek mind rejects this.** The law of non-contradiction: A cannot be both B and not-B in the same sense at the same time.

**The Hebrew mind embraces this.** God's truth exceeds our logic.

## Peshat and Derash

**Peshat** (פְּשָׁט) - Simple, plain meaning
What the text says on the surface.

**Derash** (דְּרַשׁ) - Interpretive meaning
What the text implies, teaches, reveals through interpretation.

**Both are valid.** Derash doesn't replace peshat. The plain meaning remains, but it's not the only meaning.

**Example - "And Abraham rose early in the morning" (Genesis 22:3):**

*Peshat:* Abraham woke up early.

*Derash:* Abraham was eager to obey God. He didn't delay or hesitate. Early rising shows zealous devotion.

Is the derash "reading into" the text? Or is it drawing out implications that are truly there?

Hebrew tradition says: If it's rooted in the text and leads to deeper understanding of God's character, it's valid derash.

## Multiple Meanings, Not Relativism

**The objection:** If the text can mean many things, doesn't that make interpretation arbitrary?

**The response:** No, because:

**1. The text constrains:**
You can't make it say anything. The words are fixed. Derash must connect to peshat.

**2. Community evaluates:**
Midrash happens in community. Rabbis debate, challenge, refine interpretations. Not everything proposed is accepted.

**3. Tradition guides:**
Past interpretations are respected. You can add new layers, but you don't reject what came before.

**4. Coherence with Torah's spirit:**
Does this interpretation align with Torah's overall message—God's character, covenant faithfulness, justice and mercy?

So it's not "anything goes." But it's also not "one correct answer."

**The Greek method** (historical-critical) asks: What did the author mean?
**The Hebrew method** (midrashic) asks: What does the text teach us?

Different questions. Different goals.

## Christian Parallels

The New Testament uses midrashic methods:

**Paul's use of allegory (Galatians 4:21-31):**
Paul allegorizes Hagar and Sarah—Hagar represents Sinai covenant, Sarah represents new covenant. He explicitly says "these things are being spoken allegorically."

Is this the "original meaning" of Genesis? No. It's midrash—Paul finds Christ and the Gospel in the old story.

**Matthew's use of typology:**
"Out of Egypt I called my son" (Hosea 11:1, referring to Israel) is applied to Jesus (Matthew 2:15).

Is this what Hosea meant? No. But Matthew sees a pattern—God's son (Israel) came out of Egypt, now God's Son (Jesus) comes out of Egypt. The text speaks again in a new key.

**Hebrews and the tabernacle:**
Hebrews reads the entire tabernacle system as pointing to Christ (Hebrews 8-10). Every detail—priesthood, sacrifices, Day of Atonement—is Christological.

Is this the original intent of Leviticus? No. But is it a valid reading of how those old institutions find fulfillment in Christ? Yes.

**This is midrashic interpretation.** The apostles weren't doing modern historical-critical exegesis. They were reading Scripture the way their Jewish teachers taught them—finding new depths, new applications, Christ hidden in the old covenant.

## The Risk: Eisegesis

**Eisegesis** = reading into the text what isn't there.
**Exegesis** = reading out of the text what is there.

Greek method fears eisegesis. It wants objective, historical, original meaning to guard against reading our ideas into Scripture.

Hebrew method acknowledges: **We always bring ourselves to the text.** Our questions, our context, our concerns. The goal isn't to become neutral (impossible) but to let the text speak to us where we are.

Midrash isn't eisegesis because it's **conscious** about what it's doing. It's saying "Here's one way to read this. Here's what this text might teach us."

Eisegesis pretends to be objective while smuggling in assumptions.
Midrash is openly interpretive—and that's honest.

## Wrestling, Not Mastering

Genesis 32: Jacob wrestles with God (or an angel—the text is ambiguous). He struggles all night. At dawn, he's wounded but blessed.

**This is the model for engaging Scripture.** Not mastering it, but wrestling with it. Not standing above it analyzing, but grappling with it until it blesses you.

**Midrash is wrestling:**
- You bring your questions, your struggles, your life
- The text resists, challenges, surprises
- You don't walk away with neat answers
- You walk away transformed, blessed, limping

"I will not let you go unless you bless me" (Genesis 32:26).

That's how to read Scripture.

## The Danger of Systematization

Greek theology systematizes Scripture—extracts propositions, builds coherent doctrines, resolves contradictions.

**The result:** We master the text. We have it figured out. We know what it says.

But can you master the living God? Can you systematize YHWH?

**Midrash resists this.** It keeps the text open, layered, inexhaustible. It refuses closure.

"Turn it and turn it, for everything is in it."

You never reach the bottom. There's always more.

Perhaps that's the point. Perhaps Scripture is meant to be inexhaustible, not mastered.

Perhaps we're meant to wrestle, not systematize.

Perhaps the text is alive—and alive things can't be pinned down.
