*Relationship* established by oath and sealed with [blood](#salvation/blood 'Hebrew')—the pattern running through all of Scripture.

Hebrew: *Berith* (ברית)—covenant, treaty, binding agreement. Not just a contract (conditional exchange), but a committed relationship.

Greek: *Diathēkē* (διαθήκη)—covenant, testament, will. In the NT, the new covenant sealed by Christ's blood.

The question: Is salvation a legal transaction (contract) or a relational bond (covenant)? Or both?

# Covenant: The Relational Framework

The Bible is not a systematic theology textbook. It's a covenant document—the story of God establishing relationship with His people through binding oaths sealed in blood.

**From Genesis to Revelation:**
- God makes covenant with Noah (Genesis 9).
- God makes covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15, 17).
- God makes covenant with Israel at Sinai (Exodus 19-24).
- God makes covenant with David (2 Samuel 7).
- God promises a New Covenant (Jeremiah 31).
- Jesus inaugurates the New Covenant in His blood (Luke 22:20).

**Salvation isn't just forgiveness of sins. It's covenant relationship restored.**

## The Meaning of "Covenant"

### Hebrew: *Berith* (ברית)

**A binding agreement, treaty, oath.**

**Root meaning:** Possibly from "to cut" (*bara*)—referring to the ritual of cutting animals to seal a covenant (Genesis 15:10).

**Key elements of a *berith*:**
1. **Parties:** Two or more individuals or groups.
2. **Obligations:** Terms each party agrees to uphold.
3. **Oath:** Solemn vow binding the parties.
4. **Witnesses:** Heaven and earth, stones, altars.
5. **Sign/Seal:** Circumcision, rainbow, Sabbath.
6. **Sanctions:** Blessings for obedience, curses for violation.
7. **Ratification:** Often by blood sacrifice.

**A covenant is more than a contract.** Contracts are transactional (I give X, you give Y). Covenants are relational (I commit myself to you).

### Greek: *Diathēkē* (διαθήκη)

**Covenant, testament, will.**

In classical Greek, *diathēkē* usually means "last will and testament"—a one-sided arrangement where the testator bequeaths inheritance.

**In the Septuagint (Greek OT):** *Diathēkē* translates Hebrew *berith*.

**In the NT:** Both meanings blend. God's covenant is:
- **One-sided** (He initiates, He swears, He keeps it).
- **But relational** (it involves us, calls for response).

**Hebrews 9:16-17:** "For where a will (*diathēkē*) is involved, the death of the one who made it must be established. For a will takes effect only at death."

Christ's death puts the New Covenant into effect—like a will that becomes valid when the testator dies.

## Covenant vs. Contract

**Similarities:**
- Both are binding agreements.
- Both have terms.
- Both can be violated.

**Differences:**

**Contract:**
- Conditional: "If you do X, I'll do Y."
- Transactional: Exchange of goods/services.
- Limited: Covers specific obligations.
- Breakable: If terms aren't met, contract is void.

**Covenant:**
- Relational: "I bind myself to you."
- Personal: Establishes kinship, loyalty, identity.
- Total: Encompasses whole life, not just specific duties.
- Binding: Even when violated, the covenant stands (though consequences follow).

**Example: Marriage**

**Contract model:** "I'll provide income, you'll cook and clean." If one party fails, the contract is void.

**Covenant model:** "I give myself to you, for better or worse, in sickness and health, till death." It's not transactional; it's total commitment.

**God's covenant is marriage-like (Hosea 2:19-20, Ephesians 5:25-32), not business-like.**

## The Structure of Ancient Near Eastern Covenants

Ancient treaties (Hittite, Assyrian) followed a pattern, and biblical covenants often mirror it:

**1. Preamble:** Identifies the parties.

**2. Historical Prologue:** Recounts what the greater party has done for the lesser.

**3. Stipulations:** Laws/obligations the vassal must obey.

**4. Witnesses:** Gods, heaven and earth.

**5. Blessings and Curses:** Consequences for obedience/disobedience.

**6. Ratification:** Ceremony sealing the covenant (often involving blood).

**Example: The Mosaic Covenant (Exodus-Deuteronomy)**

- **Preamble:** "I am the LORD your God" (Exodus 20:2a).
- **Historical Prologue:** "Who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery" (Exodus 20:2b).
- **Stipulations:** The Ten Commandments and the Law (Exodus 20-23, Leviticus, Deuteronomy).
- **Witnesses:** "I call heaven and earth to witness against you today" (Deuteronomy 30:19).
- **Blessings and Curses:** Deuteronomy 28.
- **Ratification:** Blood sprinkled on the altar and people (Exodus 24:6-8).

**The structure shows:** God's covenant is based on [grace](#salvation/grace 'Hebrew') ("I brought you out") *before* law ("therefore obey"). Relationship precedes obligation.

## Major Biblical Covenants

### 1. The Noahic Covenant (Genesis 9:8-17)

**Parties:** God and all creation (Noah, his descendants, every living creature).

**Promise:** "Never again shall all flesh be cut off by the waters of the flood" (Genesis 9:11).

**Obligations:** Humanity must not eat blood (Genesis 9:4) and must uphold justice (Genesis 9:5-6).

**Sign:** The rainbow.

**Nature:** Unconditional. God swears He will preserve creation. He doesn't promise Noah won't sin—He promises the earth won't be destroyed again by flood.

**Scope:** Universal. All humanity, all creation.

### 2. The Abrahamic Covenant (Genesis 12, 15, 17)

**Parties:** God and Abraham (and his descendants).

**Promises:**
- **Land:** "To your offspring I will give this land" (Genesis 12:7).
- **Descendants:** "I will make of you a great nation" (Genesis 12:2).
- **Blessing:** "In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3).

**Ratification (Genesis 15:9-18):** God alone passes through the cut animals. Abraham is asleep. **God swears the oath unilaterally.** He binds Himself.

**Sign:** Circumcision (Genesis 17:10-14).

**Nature:** Unconditional (God's promises don't depend on Abraham's performance). But obedience is expected (Genesis 17:1: "Walk before me, and be blameless").

**New Testament fulfillment:**
- **Land:** The whole earth (Romans 4:13).
- **Descendants:** All who believe, Jew and Gentile (Galatians 3:29).
- **Blessing:** Salvation through Christ (Galatians 3:8, 14).

### 3. The Mosaic Covenant (Exodus 19-24)

**Parties:** God and Israel.

**Basis:** God's [redemption](#salvation/redemption 'Hebrew') of Israel from Egypt (Exodus 20:2).

**Stipulations:** The Law—moral (Ten Commandments), civil (governance), ceremonial (worship, sacrifices).

**Ratification:** Moses sprinkles blood on the altar (representing God) and on the people (Exodus 24:6-8). "Behold the blood of the covenant that the LORD has made with you."

**Nature:** Conditional. "If you will indeed obey my voice and keep my covenant, you shall be my treasured possession" (Exodus 19:5).

**But:** Even when Israel broke covenant, God didn't abandon them. He judged, exiled, but promised restoration (Deuteronomy 30:1-10).

**The tension:** The Mosaic Covenant is conditional (obey or face curses), yet God's faithfulness endures despite Israel's failure.

**New Testament perspective:**
- The Law reveals sin (Romans 3:20), drives us to Christ (Galatians 3:24).
- Christ fulfills the Law (Matthew 5:17).
- Believers are not *under* the Law's condemnation (Romans 6:14) but walk in its righteousness by the Spirit (Romans 8:4).

### 4. The Davidic Covenant (2 Samuel 7:8-16)

**Parties:** God and David (and his royal line).

**Promise:** "Your throne shall be established forever" (2 Samuel 7:16).

**Nature:** Unconditional. God swears to David that his dynasty will never end.

**Psalm 89:3-4:** "I have made a covenant with my chosen one; I have sworn to David my servant: 'I will establish your offspring forever, and build your throne for all generations.'"

**Problem:** The Davidic dynasty *did* end (Babylonian exile, 586 BC). No Davidic king ruled Israel after the exile.

**Solution:** The covenant is fulfilled in [Jesus](#jesus/messiah 'Hebrew'), David's descendant, whose kingdom is eternal.

**Luke 1:32-33:** "The Lord God will give to him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of his kingdom there will be no end."

### 5. The New Covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34)

**The promise:**

"Behold, the days are coming, declares the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers on the day when I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt, my covenant that they broke... For this is the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel after those days, declares the LORD: I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts. And I will be their God, and they shall be my people... For I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more."

**Key differences from the Mosaic Covenant:**
- **Internalized:** Law written on hearts, not stone.
- **Universal knowledge of God:** "They shall all know me, from the least of them to the greatest."
- **Complete forgiveness:** "I will remember their sin no more."

**New Testament fulfillment:**

**Luke 22:20:** "This cup that is poured out for you is the new covenant in my blood."

Christ's death inaugurates the New Covenant.

**Hebrews 8:6-13:** The New Covenant is superior to the Old. It's based on better promises, mediated by a better high priest (Jesus), and accomplishes what the Old couldn't—transformation of the heart.

**2 Corinthians 3:6:** "God... has made us sufficient to be ministers of a new covenant, not of the letter but of the Spirit. For the letter kills, but the Spirit gives life."

**The Spirit is the agent of the New Covenant.** He writes the law on hearts (Ezekiel 36:26-27), enables obedience, guarantees inheritance (Ephesians 1:13-14).

## Covenant and Relationship

**Why does covenant matter?**

Because **salvation is not just legal status change**—it's relationship restored.

**The problem of sin:**

**Greek framework ([justification](#salvation/justification 'Greek')):** Sin makes us legally guilty. The solution: forensic pardon.

**Hebrew framework (covenant):** Sin breaks covenant relationship. The solution: restoration of the bond.

**Both are biblical. But covenant adds relational depth.**

### Marriage as Covenant

**Malachi 2:14:** "The LORD was witness between you and the wife of your youth, to whom you have been faithless, though she is your companion and your wife by covenant."

Marriage is covenant. It's not just a contract ("I'll do X if you do Y"). It's a binding relational commitment.

**When one spouse is unfaithful, the covenant is violated.** But the wronged spouse can choose to forgive and restore the relationship—or dissolve it.

**God as the faithful spouse:**

**Hosea:** God commands Hosea to marry Gomer, a prostitute, to illustrate Israel's unfaithfulness and God's faithful love.

**Hosea 2:19-20:** "And I will betroth you to me forever. I will betroth you to me in righteousness and in justice, in steadfast love and in mercy. I will betroth you to me in faithfulness. And you shall know the LORD."

Israel broke covenant (spiritual adultery—idolatry). God could have divorced them (Jeremiah 3:8). But He promises to restore the marriage.

**The New Covenant is remarriage.** God brings His people back, writes the law on their hearts, and renews the bond.

### Father and Children

**Exodus 4:22-23:** "Thus says the LORD, Israel is my firstborn son, and I say to you, 'Let my son go that he may serve me.'"

Covenant creates family relationship. Israel is God's son.

**2 Corinthians 6:18:** "And I will be a father to you, and you shall be sons and daughters to me, says the Lord Almighty."

**The New Covenant brings adoption:** We're children of God (Romans 8:15, Galatians 4:5-7).

**Salvation isn't just pardon from a judge. It's adoption into the family.**

### Suzerain and Vassal

Ancient Near Eastern treaties were between a suzerain (great king) and a vassal (lesser king). The suzerain protected the vassal; the vassal pledged loyalty.

**God as Suzerain, Israel as vassal:**

God delivers Israel (Exodus), makes covenant (Sinai), and promises protection. Israel pledges exclusive loyalty ("You shall have no other gods," Exodus 20:3).

**The New Covenant:** Jesus is the mediator. We're the vassal—pledging allegiance to King Jesus, who conquered sin and death and now protects us.

## Blood and Covenant

**Covenants are ratified with blood.**

### Why Blood?

**Leviticus 17:11:** "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life."

**Blood represents life.** Pouring out blood = giving life.

**Covenant ratified by blood means:** "My life is bound to this oath. If I break it, may my life be poured out like this animal's."

### Examples

**Genesis 15:9-18:** God makes covenant with Abraham. Animals are cut in half. Normally, both parties walk through the pieces (symbolizing, "If I break this covenant, may I be cut in half like these animals"). But God alone passes through (as a smoking fire pot and flaming torch). God swears the oath; Abraham doesn't. **God binds Himself.**

**Exodus 24:6-8:** Moses sprinkles blood on the altar (God's presence) and on the people. **The blood connects God and Israel in covenant bond.**

**Hebrews 9:18-22:** "Therefore not even the first covenant was inaugurated without blood... Indeed, under the law almost everything is purified with blood, and without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness of sins."

### The New Covenant Blood

**Matthew 26:27-28:** "And he took a cup, and when he had given thanks he gave it to them, saying, 'Drink of it, all of you, for this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins.'"

**Hebrews 9:14:** "How much more will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without blemish to God, purify our conscience from dead works to serve the living God."

**Christ's blood seals the New Covenant.** Not the blood of bulls and goats (Hebrews 10:4), but His own life poured out.

**Hebrews 12:24:** "Jesus, the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel."

Abel's blood cried out for vengeance (Genesis 4:10). Christ's blood speaks forgiveness, peace, reconciliation.

## Conditional vs. Unconditional Covenants

**Are God's covenants conditional or unconditional?**

### Unconditional (Promissory)

**God swears to do something regardless of human response.**

**Examples:**
- **Noahic:** God will preserve creation.
- **Abrahamic:** God will bless Abraham's descendants and give them the land.
- **Davidic:** God will establish David's throne forever.
- **New:** God will forgive sins and write the law on hearts.

**These are one-sided.** God promises. God fulfills. Human failure doesn't void them.

**Romans 11:29:** "For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable."

**The basis:** God's character, not human performance.

### Conditional (Obligation)

**God promises blessing if the people obey, curses if they disobey.**

**Example:**
- **Mosaic:** "If you obey, blessings. If you disobey, curses" (Deuteronomy 28).

**Leviticus 26:14-15:** "But if you will not listen to me and will not do all these commandments... I will do this to you: I will visit you with panic..."

**Israel's history:** Disobedience led to exile. Obedience (under judges, kings like David and Hezekiah) brought peace.

### The Tension

**How can covenants be both conditional and unconditional?**

**Resolution:**

The *relationship* is unconditional (God doesn't abandon His people permanently). The *blessings* within the relationship are conditional (obedience brings flourishing; disobedience brings discipline).

**Analogy: Parent and child.**

The parent-child relationship is permanent (unconditional). But the child's experience depends on behavior—obedience brings joy, disobedience brings discipline.

**Similarly:** God's covenant commitment to His people is unbreakable. But how they experience that covenant varies—faithfulness brings blessing, unfaithfulness brings chastening (yet not abandonment).

## Covenant and the Gospel

**How does covenant shape our understanding of salvation?**

### Justification Through Covenant

**Traditional view:** Justification is forensic—God declares us righteous in the courtroom.

**Covenant view (N.T. Wright, others):** Justification is God's declaration that we're members of His covenant people.

**Not:** "You're morally righteous" (we're not).

**But:** "You're in the covenant. You belong to the family."

**Romans 4:** Abraham was justified by [faith](#salvation/faith 'Drill')—he believed God's covenant promise.

**Galatians 3:29:** "If you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's offspring, heirs according to promise."

**Justification brings us into the Abrahamic covenant.** We're counted as Abraham's children, recipients of the promise.

**Both views are true:** Justification is forensic (legal standing) *and* covenantal (family membership). Not either/or, but both/and.

### Sanctification as Covenant Faithfulness

**Sanctification** isn't just moral improvement. It's learning to live as covenant people.

**Leviticus 20:26:** "You shall be holy to me, for I the LORD am holy and have separated you from the peoples, that you should be mine."

Holiness is covenant identity. You're set apart, distinct, belonging to God.

**1 Peter 2:9:** "But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession."

**Sanctification is embodying your covenant status.**

### Perseverance Through Covenant Promises

**Can we lose salvation?** (See [perseverance](#salvation/perseverance 'Greek').)

**Covenant perspective:** God keeps covenant even when we fail.

**Psalm 89:30-34:** "If [David's] children forsake my law... then I will punish their transgression with the rod... but I will not remove from him my steadfast love or be false to my covenant."

Discipline, yes. Abandonment, no.

**Hebrews 13:5:** "I will never leave you nor forsake you."

God's covenant faithfulness guarantees believers' [perseverance](#salvation/perseverance 'Greek').

## Covenant Renewal

**When Israel broke covenant, God didn't discard them. He called them to renew.**

**Joshua 24:** Joshua gathers Israel at Shechem and calls them to renew their covenant commitment. "Choose this day whom you will serve" (v. 15).

**2 Kings 23:** King Josiah discovers the Book of the Law, realizes Israel has violated covenant, and leads the people in covenant renewal.

**Nehemiah 9-10:** After returning from exile, Ezra reads the Law, the people confess sin, and they renew covenant.

**The pattern:** Covenant broken → repentance → renewal.

**For Christians:**

The Lord's Supper is covenant renewal. We remember Christ's death (the blood of the New Covenant) and reaffirm our commitment.

**1 Corinthians 11:25-26:** "This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me. For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes."

## The Eternal Covenant

**Hebrews 13:20:** "Now may the God of peace who brought again from the dead our Lord Jesus, the great shepherd of the sheep, by the blood of the eternal covenant..."

The New Covenant is eternal. It won't be replaced. It won't fail.

**Revelation 21:3:** "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God."

**The ultimate covenant fulfillment:** God dwelling with His people forever. No more sin, no more separation, no more broken promises.

**This is the goal of all covenants from the beginning:** God with His people.

## Covenant and the Trinity

**The covenant of redemption (traditional Reformed theology):**

Before creation, the Father, Son, and Spirit made a covenant to save the elect.

**Basis:**
- **Psalm 2:7-8:** The Father says to the Son, "You are my Son... Ask of me, and I will make the nations your heritage."
- **John 17:6, 9:** Jesus prays, "I have manifested your name to the people whom you gave me out of the world. Yours they were, and you gave them to me... I am praying for them. I am not praying for the world but for those whom you have given me."

**The idea:** The Father promised to give the Son a people. The Son agreed to redeem them. The Spirit agreed to apply that redemption.

**Covenant structure:**
- **Parties:** Father, Son, Spirit.
- **Promise:** The Father gives the elect to the Son.
- **Obligation:** The Son redeems them by His death.
- **Sealing:** The Spirit applies salvation.

**Critique:** Not explicitly taught in Scripture. More theological inference.

**But:** It captures the biblical emphasis that salvation is God's plan from eternity (Ephesians 1:4-5), grounded in the triune God's commitment to save.

## Covenant: Relational, Not Merely Legal

**Greek systematic theology emphasizes forensic categories:** Sin as lawbreaking, justification as legal pardon.

**Hebrew covenant theology emphasizes relational categories:** Sin as covenant breaking, salvation as relationship restored.

**Both are biblical.**

But **covenant adds warmth.** Salvation isn't just being acquitted in court. It's being brought into the family, married to the King, adopted as God's child.

**The courtroom declares us not guilty.**

**The covenant brings us home.**

And that home is eternal—God with His people, forever.

"I will be their God, and they shall be my people" (Jeremiah 31:33).
