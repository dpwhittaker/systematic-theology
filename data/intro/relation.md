Theology as *relationship*, not information. The Hebrew Scriptures don't ask "What is God?" but "Who is God *to us*?" The question is relational from the start.

Israel's identity: "We are the people God brought out of Egypt." Not "We are humans with souls," but "We are the ones He chose, delivered, made promises to."

Names matter because they encode relationships. God reveals Himself as "I AM WHO I AM" (*Ehyeh asher ehyeh*)—presence, not definition. Later: "The God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." Not a list of attributes, but a history of encounters.

Knowledge comes through engagement. You don't *know* God by studying Him from a distance—you know Him by [walking](#intro/wrestling 'Hebrew') with Him, [arguing](#intro/wrestling 'Hebrew') with Him, obeying and rebelling and returning. Like a marriage, not a thesis.

Greek philosophy seeks truth through detached observation. Hebrew faith seeks truth through covenant faithfulness. One says "step back to see clearly." The other says "step in to truly know."

# Covenant vs Contract {#covenant-vs-contract}

The Hebrew *berith* (covenant) is fundamentally different from a Greek legal contract:

**Contract** (Greek mindset):
- Exchange of goods/services
- Based on mutual benefit
- Conditional: "If you do X, I'll do Y"
- Parties remain autonomous
- Breakable if terms violated
- Focuses on obligations

**Covenant** (Hebrew mindset):
- Creation of family bond
- Based on committed love
- Unconditional promise: "I will be your God"
- Parties become one ("bone of my bone")
- Unbreakable even when violated (see Hosea)
- Focuses on relationship

When God makes covenant with Abraham, He doesn't negotiate terms—He pledges Himself. "I will make you a great nation... all peoples will be blessed through you" (Genesis 12:2-3). Abraham receives promises, not a contract.

The covenant is sealed in *blood*—not as payment, but as shared life. Animals cut in two, God walking between the pieces (Genesis 15)—a visual oath: "May I be torn apart if I break this promise." Later, circumcision marks covenant members in their own flesh.

This is *relational* language, not *legal*. God doesn't hire Israel as employees—He adopts them as children. The Law (Torah) isn't a contract's fine print—it's family instruction, "how we live because of who we belong to."

When Israel violates covenant, God doesn't simply void the agreement. He *grieves*. He pursues. He disciplines as a father, not sues as a creditor. Hosea pictures God as a betrayed husband who *still* loves his unfaithful wife, *still* seeks reconciliation.

Can systematic theology—built on Greek foundations—capture a faith rooted in covenant relationship?
