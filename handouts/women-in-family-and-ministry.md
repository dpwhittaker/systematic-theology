# Women in Family and Ministry: The Differences That Remain

Once the obvious cultural exegesis is done — once we've granted that 1 Corinthians 14 addresses uneducated women disrupting a synagogue-style assembly, that 1 Timothy 2 is written into the shadow of the Artemis cult at Ephesus, that "submission" in the household codes mirrors Greco-Roman manuals being deliberately *subverted* by Paul, that *kephalē* often means "source" rather than "boss" — once all of that is on the table, the egalitarian conclusion is largely secure. Women are co-heirs, co-prophets, co-laborers. Junia is an apostle. Phoebe is a deacon and patron. Priscilla teaches Apollos. Deborah judges Israel. Huldah validates Scripture for a king. The Spirit is poured out on sons *and daughters* (Joel 2:28; Acts 2:17).

But that is not the whole picture, and pretending it is requires us to suppress half the text. Even after the most generous modern reading, **a residue of asymmetry remains** — not as denigration, not as a ceiling on women's gifts, but as a real, theologically-grounded distinction between how the sexes are meant to live out the image of God. This handout maps that residue: the passages and patterns that survive the cultural-context lens and still describe men and women as *equal in dignity but distinct in calling*.

A note before we begin. Partway through this study an observation emerged that materially changes how some of the cornerstone passages should be read — specifically, that the Pastoral Epistles' creation-order arguments may themselves be polemical corrections of a *specific* Ephesian myth (a proto-gnostic Eve-first cosmology fed by the Artemis cult), not free-standing systematic theology. The case for that reading rests on a methodological move that mainstream scholarship is inconsistent about applying. **§V (the Interlude) makes that case explicit, and the sections it touches have been written with that reading in view.** The residue *does* still survive — but it is narrower and more carefully described than the older complementarian framing would have it.

The order is rough-to-precise. Each section asks: *after everything we've granted, what is left?*

## I. Creation Asymmetry

*Genesis 1 establishes equality. Genesis 2 establishes asymmetry. Both are part of the same canonical witness, and the second cannot be reduced to a footnote on the first. The man is formed first, names the animals, and recognizes his own incompleteness before the woman is brought to him. She is built from his side — not from the ground — and given as ezer kenegdo. Even after we correctly translate ezer ("strong help," used elsewhere of God Himself, not "subordinate assistant"), the structure of the narrative still places the woman as the one given to the man, not the man to the woman.*

1. **Image-bearing is shared** (Gen 1:27) — "So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them." The image is co-extensive with humanity. Neither sex carries it more fully than the other.

2. **The dominion mandate is jointly given** (Gen 1:28) — "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it." No division of labor specified. Both are commissioned to rule.

3. **The order of formation** (Gen 2:7, 21–22) — The man is formed from dust; the woman is built from the man's side. This is not equality of *source*. Paul explicitly notices this and weaponizes it theologically (1 Tim 2:13; 1 Cor 11:8–9), and it cannot be exegeted away as a later imposition — it is *in the text Paul is reading*.

4. **The naming** (Gen 2:23) — "She shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man." Naming, in the Hebrew narrative grammar, is an act of authority. Adam names the animals (2:19–20) and then names the woman. After the fall he names her again (Eve, 3:20). The wife-as-named, husband-as-namer pattern is established at creation, not at the fall.

5. **Ezer kenegdo — "the help that opposes"** (Gen 2:18) — Usually rendered "a helper fit for him," but both halves of the phrase carry sharper weight than English captures. *Ezer* is a strong word — used elsewhere of God Himself coming to Israel's military aid (Ps 33:20, 70:5, 121:1–2; Ex 18:4) — so "helper" does not mean "subordinate assistant." *Kenegdo* is built on *neged*, "in front of, over against, opposite, facing." It is the same root used for an adversary standing across the battle line. The fused phrase therefore means something closer to *"a rescuing strength that stands opposite him"* — a counterweight, not a complement in the weak modern sense. The man as God forms him is incomplete on his own: left alone, his drive to subdue and fill the earth tilts toward raw conquest, toward dominion without relation. He needs a force that *opposes* that tilt — not by submitting to it, but by pulling against it with a counter-current of relational longing, attunement, and trust. Eve is given to Adam not to second his motions but to *resist* them where they need resisting. The asymmetry is still real (she is made *for* his need; the text never reverses the direction), but the help itself is dialectical, not deferential. The Hebrew imagines a marriage shaped less like commander-and-aide and more like two forces in tension whose opposition produces fruitfulness.

6. **The one-flesh union** (Gen 2:24) — "A man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife." The active verb is the man's. He leaves, he holds fast. Even where the result is mutual ("they shall become one flesh"), the initiating motion is described from the husband's side.

7. **The fall's asymmetric address** (Gen 3:9, 16–19) — God seeks out the man first ("Where are you?") even though the woman ate first. The man is held representatively responsible (compare Rom 5:12 — "sin entered the world through *one man*," not through Eve). The woman's curse touches childbearing and marital dynamics ("your desire shall be for your husband, and he shall rule over you"); the man's curse touches work and the ground.

**What survives the cultural lens:** Even if every later "wives submit" passage were dissolved into first-century context, Genesis 2 stands behind them. Paul does not invoke Roman household codes when he grounds male headship; he invokes *creation order*. That move is available to him only because the Genesis text actually says what he says it says. But the same Genesis text that grounds the asymmetry also defines its shape: the woman is *ezer kenegdo* — strength that stands opposite. The man-without-the-woman is not the default ruler with an optional companion; he is the diagnosed-incomplete creature who cannot fulfill the dominion mandate without a counterweight. Headship in this frame is not the right to command an assistant; it is the responsibility to lead a partnership that includes a force built specifically to resist the worst tendencies of male rule.

## II. Headship in Marriage

*Ephesians 5 is the headship text most reshaped by modern scholarship — and rightly so. "Wives submit to your husbands" is preceded by "submit to one another out of reverence for Christ" (v. 21), and the husband's command is not "lead her" or "rule her" but "love her as Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her" — sacrificial love unto death. The Greco-Roman paterfamilias would have been scandalized by the husband's side of this code; Paul is dismantling male tyranny, not endorsing it. Granted. And yet, after all that work, the text still calls the husband the "head" (kephalē) of the wife, and still tells the wife to submit "as to the Lord." The mutual-submission frame does not erase the asymmetric description that follows it — it qualifies it.*

1. **Ephesians 5:22–24** — "Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife even as Christ is the head of the church, his body, and is himself its Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything."

2. **Ephesians 5:25–33** — "Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her... In the same way husbands should love their wives as their own bodies... let each one of you love his wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband."

3. **Colossians 3:18–19** — "Wives, submit to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord. Husbands, love your wives, and do not be harsh with them." A second, independent witness with the same asymmetry. Whatever Paul means, he means it twice.

4. **1 Peter 3:1–7** — "Wives, be subject to your own husbands, so that even if some do not obey the word, they may be won without a word by the conduct of their wives... Likewise, husbands, live with your wives in an understanding way, showing honor to the woman as the weaker vessel, since they are heirs with you of the grace of life." Three things to notice: *(a)* "weaker vessel" — sometimes softened to "more delicate" but it is a real description, and almost certainly refers in part to physical strength; *(b)* "heirs with you of the grace of life" — full spiritual co-inheritance, unambiguous; *(c)* the asymmetry of "be subject" / "live with in an understanding way" is preserved alongside the equality.

5. **1 Corinthians 11:3** — "The head of every man is Christ, the head of a wife is her husband, and the head of Christ is God." Even if *kephalē* means "source" rather than "authority" (a contested but defensible reading), the analogy is exact: as Christ relates to God the Father, so the wife relates to her husband. The Father–Son relation is *equal in essence, asymmetric in role and procession*. That is precisely the residue this handout is trying to name.

6. **Titus 2:4–5** — Older women are to train younger women "to love their husbands and children, to be self-controlled, pure, working at home, kind, and submissive to their own husbands, that the word of God may not be reviled." The submission language sits alongside an active, practical, household-economic role — not silence and seclusion.

**What survives the cultural lens:** Mutual submission (5:21) does not erase the named head/body asymmetry of 5:22–33. The husband is given a harder command (die for her) and the wife is given a sharper one (submit to him); both are oriented toward the Christ–church icon. Removing the asymmetry destroys the icon. The marriage relationship is meant to *picture* the gospel, with the husband enacting Christ's self-giving and the wife enacting the church's trust. This is not interchangeable; the picture requires distinct roles.

But "submission" here cannot be read against the grain of Genesis 2. The wife Paul is addressing is still *ezer kenegdo* — the strength that stands opposite. Her submission is the submission of a counterweight, not of an aide. She is not commanded to suppress the very opposition God built her to provide; she is commanded to render it inside a relationship of trust rather than rivalry. The husband, for his part, is not commanded to overrule that opposition; he is commanded to die for the woman whose resistance keeps him honest. Headship-as-tyranny is precisely the form of male rule the *kenegdo* counterweight exists to oppose, and it is the form Paul's sacrificial-love command targets. The marriage Paul describes is one in which the husband leads by self-giving and the wife trusts by leaning in *while still pulling against him* where his conquest instincts need pulling against. That tension is not a flaw in the design; it is the design.

## III. Childbearing and Embodied Difference

*Some asymmetries do not require exegesis at all — they are written into the body. Only women bear children, only women nurse them, and the early months and years of human life are physically and chemically bound to the mother in a way no theology of "interchangeable roles" can dissolve. The Bible takes this seriously; modern egalitarian theology often does not. The result is a theology that pretends biology is a social construction — a profoundly Greek (gnostic) move dressed up as progress.*

1. **The pain of childbirth** (Gen 3:16) — "I will surely multiply your pain in childbearing; in pain you shall bring forth children." The curse touches both sexes asymmetrically, and the woman's curse is located in the one function only she performs. This is not an arbitrary punishment; it is a recognition that bringing forth life is uniquely hers, and now uniquely costly.

2. **"Saved through childbearing"** (1 Tim 2:15) — "Yet she will be saved through childbearing — if they continue in faith and love and holiness, with self-control." The verse is famously difficult; readings range from "saved through *the* childbearing" (i.e., through Christ's birth) to "preserved through the danger of childbirth" to "lives out her vocation through motherhood." All three readings share one thing: Paul treats *childbearing* as theologically dense in a way that has no male parallel. He never tells men they are "saved through" any sex-specific function.

3. **Titus 2:3–5** — Older women teach younger women specifically about loving husbands, loving children, and managing the home. Paul does not tell Titus to set up a parallel curriculum for men on fatherhood and home management; the maternal vocation is named as its own discipleship track.

4. **Proverbs 31:10–31** — The *eshet chayil* ("valiant woman") is celebrated in specifically female terms: she runs an enterprise, buys fields, clothes her household, opens her hand to the poor, and "her children rise up and call her blessed." This is not a generic "excellent person" passage; it is a doxology to a distinctly female form of strength.

5. **The maternal images of God** — God is described as a mother bear (Hos 13:8), a mother hen (Matt 23:37), a woman in labor (Isa 42:14), a nursing mother (Isa 49:15). These exist. But they are *images* God borrows; the consistent grammatical naming of God is masculine (Father, King, Husband, Bridegroom, Son). The asymmetry of the imagery is itself part of the data.

6. **Mary and the Magnificat** (Luke 1:46–55) — The Incarnation passes through a woman's *yes* and a woman's body. The most consequential act of cooperation with God in human history is a maternal one. Mary is not an apostle, not an elder, not a teacher of public theology — she is the *theotokos*, and the entire New Testament is built on her unique contribution. The Reformation rightly resists Marian excess, but should not erase Marian distinctiveness.

7. **The widows' order** (1 Tim 5:9–10) — Paul institutes a recognized ecclesial role for older widows who have raised children, shown hospitality, washed the feet of the saints, and devoted themselves to good works. This is a *female-specific* ministry order, with female-specific qualifications.

**What survives the cultural lens:** The maternal vocation is not a culturally contingent gender stereotype Paul could have written around if he had been less patriarchal. It is a biological-theological reality the New Testament treats as theologically loaded. Any account of women's equality that requires childbearing to be either invisible or a hindrance to "real" ministry is more Greek than biblical.

## IV. The Teaching and Authority Restriction

*This is the most-litigated text in the egalitarian/complementarian debate, and the section where the §V Interlude does the most work. The conventional complementarian reading takes Paul's "Adam was formed first, then Eve" as a free-standing creation-order argument, not bound to Ephesus, intended to apply across all times and places. That reading was the one the first draft of this handout commended. The Interlude (§V) gives reasons to reframe it: the same creation-order language reads very differently if Paul is correcting an Ephesian cult-formed teaching that put Eve first. On that reading, "Adam was formed first" is not a free-standing axiom but an anti-cult corrective — Paul reaching for Genesis to disarm a specific myth. Both readings are walked through below.*

1. **1 Timothy 2:11–14** — "Let a woman learn quietly with all submissiveness. I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man; rather, she is to remain quiet. For Adam was formed first, then Eve; and Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor."

2. **1 Corinthians 14:33–35** — "The women should keep silent in the churches. For they are not permitted to speak, but should be in submission, as the Law also says. If there is anything they desire to learn, let them ask their husbands at home."

3. **1 Corinthians 11:4–6** — Women *do* pray and prophesy in the assembly (the text assumes it), but with their heads covered — a visible marker of the husband–wife order being honored *even as* the woman exercises a vocal ministry. The text simultaneously affirms women's public prophetic voice and a visible sign of asymmetry.

4. **The appeal to creation — and what it might be correcting** — In 1 Tim 2:13 Paul's stated reason is *Adam was formed first, then Eve*. Older readings (including the original draft of this handout) treated that as a free-standing structural argument from creation order, intended to outlast Ephesus. A growing body of evidence reframes it: the Ephesian opposition Paul is fighting may have been teaching the *opposite* — an Eve-first cosmology in which the woman is the primal being and the source of the man, fed by the Artemis cult's mother-goddess imagery and the proto-gnostic streams later attested at Nag Hammadi (Apocryphon of John, Hypostasis of the Archons, On the Origin of the World — texts in which Eve is the "instructress" who awakens Adam). On this reading, Paul's "Adam was formed first" is not a creation-order axiom but a *direct rebuttal* of a specific false teaching circulating among the women he is restricting. He reaches for Genesis because Genesis is the weapon that disarms the myth. (See §V below for the dating evidence.)

5. **The appeal to the fall — same logic** — "The woman was deceived" (1 Tim 2:14) becomes legible inside the same polemic. Several of the later Gnostic Eve-traditions treat Eve's encounter with the serpent as *enlightenment* — the moment she received saving gnosis. Paul names the same scene and calls it *deception*. He is not making a generic claim that women are gullible; he is contradicting a specific cult-formed narrative in which Eve's "knowledge" from the serpent is the foundation of female spiritual priority.

6. **Women prophets in both Testaments** — Miriam (Ex 15:20), Deborah (Judg 4–5), Huldah (2 Kings 22), Anna (Luke 2:36), Philip's four daughters (Acts 21:9), the prophesying women of 1 Cor 11. Prophecy — speaking the word of the Lord under direct inspiration — is *not* restricted. The restriction Paul names is on *teaching with authority* over men in the assembly, which is a narrower category.

7. **"Saved through childbearing" inside the same polemic** (1 Tim 2:15) — Several of the same proto-gnostic streams disparaged marriage and childbirth (1 Tim 4:3, in the same letter, names opponents who "forbid marriage"). Paul's "she will be saved through childbearing" reads in that light as another anti-cult correction: motherhood is not the spiritual disqualification the false teachers were calling it; it is integral to the woman's vocation, not the encumbrance the ascetic-mythic stream was treating it as.

**What survives the cultural lens — revised:** Less than the older complementarian reading claimed, and the revision matters. If the 1 Tim 2 restriction is occasion-specific anti-cult correction rather than a context-free creation-order rule, then Paul's "I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man" cannot be lifted out of Ephesus and applied as a universal prohibition. What survives is narrower:

- The *form* of Paul's argument still uses Genesis. Even as polemic, he chose creation order as his counter-weapon, which means the Genesis text actually grounds the kind of distinction he is making — otherwise the counter-myth would not be refuted by appealing to it
- The *prophesying-women / 1 Cor 11* asymmetry stands: women speak publicly, but with a visible sign of the husband–wife order being honored. This passage is not Pastorals-occasion-bound; it is written to Corinth and assumes women's vocal ministry
- The distinction between *Spirit-given prophetic speech* (open to both) and *settled doctrinal teaching with governing authority* (something Paul restricts in Ephesus and at minimum patterns elsewhere) is real, but the *universality* of the second-half restriction is now genuinely in doubt

The most defensible position: Paul, in Ephesus, restricts a specific teaching-authority role *as a corrective against a specific cult-formed false teaching*. Whether that restriction generalizes to all settled eldership across all centuries is a question that cannot be settled from 1 Tim 2 alone. It has to be settled — if at all — from the broader apostolic pattern (§VI) and the office of pastor-elder itself (§VII), both of which look different once read through this lens.

## V. Interlude: The Dating Double Standard

*Before turning to the eldership pattern, a methodological aside that materially affects what comes next. The strongest objection to reading the Pastoral Epistles as anti-cult polemic has always been: "the Gnostic Eve-traditions that would make this reading work are second to fourth century. You cannot use them as evidence for what was being taught at Ephesus in the 50s or 60s." That objection sounds decisive — until it is laid next to how the same scholarship treats rabbinic literature.*

**The Mishnah was redacted around 200 CE.** The Talmud Yerushalmi was completed around 400; the Bavli around 500–600. Yet no responsible New Testament scholar refuses to use these texts to reconstruct the religious world of Jesus and Paul. Hillel and Shammai (late first century BCE), Gamaliel I (mid-first century, Paul's own teacher), Yohanan ben Zakkai (post-70), and dozens of named *tannaim* are quoted in compilations centuries later than they lived — and mainstream scholarship treats their attributed sayings as substantially preserving real first-century positions. Strack-Stemberger's standard handbook accepts this. Every major commentary on the Sermon on the Mount uses Hillelite-Shammaite debates as 1st-c. background. Pharisaism, halakhic dispute, mikveh practice, synagogue liturgy — all reconstructed by retrojecting from texts compiled a century or more later.

**The Nag Hammadi corpus was buried around 350 and composed mostly in the 2nd and 3rd centuries.** Yet when these texts attribute their cosmologies to apostolic-era figures — when their content overlaps strikingly with traditions Paul himself names as already present in Ephesus — scholarship defaults to *refusing* the same retrojection. The result is an asymmetric methodological floor: rabbinic traditions are presumed early until proven late; Gnostic traditions are presumed late until proven early. There is no principled reason for the asymmetry, and once it is named, several scholarly habits look more like inertia than evidence.

**What the 1st-c. record itself attests at Ephesus and in its orbit:**

1. **"Falsely-called knowledge"** (1 Tim 6:20) — *pseudōnymos gnōsis*. Paul names the opposition by the term ("gnosis") that the later Gnostic systems explicitly use of themselves. This is not a generic warning; it is a target name.

2. **"Myths and endless genealogies"** (1 Tim 1:4; 4:7; Titus 1:14; 3:9) — the exact generic term that later applies to the aeon-emanation genealogies of Sethian and Valentinian texts.

3. **Anti-marriage, anti-food asceticism** (1 Tim 4:3) — opponents who "forbid marriage and require abstinence from foods" — the exact ascetic-dualist signature that flowers in 2nd-c. Gnostic systems.

4. **Docetism already at Ephesus by the 90s** (1 John 4:1–3; 2 John 7) — the Johannine epistles fight a Christology that denies Christ came "in the flesh," a position that becomes a hallmark of 2nd-c. Gnostic Christology.

5. **Cerinthus** — late 1st-c. teacher in Ephesus per Irenaeus, with a Demiurge cosmology and adoptionist Christology. Recognizably proto-gnostic, contemporaneous with John.

6. **Nicolaitans** (Rev 2:6, 15) — at Ephesus and Pergamum. Patristic writers (Irenaeus, Clement, Hippolytus) link them to proto-gnostic sexual-mystical teaching.

7. **Simon Magus** (Acts 8:9–24) — named by Justin Martyr and Irenaeus as headwaters of the Gnostic stream. Acts itself attests a magos with messianic pretensions and a female consort (Helena, later cast in the tradition as Sophia/Ennoia incarnate).

**The conclusion that retrojection logic actually warrants:**

Every doctrinal feature that later coalesces into developed Gnosticism — a self-named "knowledge" tradition, myth-laden genealogies, ascetic anti-marriage praxis, docetic Christology, Demiurge cosmology, female-empowering mystical streams — is *already attested as present and being fought* in the 1st-century texts of Ephesus and its region. The 2nd–4th c. Gnostic compilations are doing for that stream roughly what the Mishnah does for the Pharisaic stream: collecting and systematizing traditions whose roots run back into the 1st century. Treating the rabbinic compilations as evidence for Jesus' world while refusing the Gnostic compilations as evidence for Paul's world is not a neutral scholarly default. It is a choice, and once named, it does not survive examination.

**What this changes:**

Once retrojection is applied evenhandedly, the historical-context case for reading 1 Tim 2 (and by extension parts of 1 Cor 11 and 1 Cor 14) as anti-cult polemic becomes substantially stronger. The Kroegers' specific lexical claim about *authentein* (that it means "to originate / claim oneself the author of") remains weak — the Baldwin database in Köstenberger and Schreiner's *Women in the Church* is a real constraint, and "to exercise authority" is the dominant attested sense. But the broader thesis — *Paul is correcting Ephesian women who were teaching a cult-formed Eve-priority myth, and reaching for Genesis to disarm it* — looks much harder to dismiss than mainstream commentary has typically allowed.

**A note on Corinth.** The same dynamic almost certainly applies to 1 Cor 11 and 1 Cor 14. Corinth was the site of the Aphrodite cult on the Acrocorinth — a fertility-goddess complex with its own female priestly tradition. The "women keep silent in the churches" passage (1 Cor 14:33–35) and the head-covering passage (1 Cor 11) plausibly address the same kind of cult-formed female religious confidence colliding with gentile men whose religious education was thinner. The retrojection logic is the same: extant Corinthian inscriptions and the Pauline letters' own internal evidence (chaotic prophetic speech, women teaching across the husband-wife order, the strange particularity of the head-covering issue) point to a religious environment shaped by the cult, not a generic Greco-Roman patriarchy that Paul is either endorsing or correcting in the abstract.

The position the rest of this handout commends, accordingly, is that the Pastorals' restrictions (and their Corinthian parallels) are *occasion-specific corrections* whose universal application is far less obvious than older readings assumed. The asymmetries that survive into the universal pattern have to be found elsewhere — in the canonical symbolic grammar, in the embodied difference of childbearing, in the Christ-bride icon of Ephesians 5 (which is not cult-polemic-bound), and in the broader apostolic pattern that the next section examines with this lens now in hand.

## VI. Eldership and the Apostolic Pattern

*Once the Pastoral Epistles are read as occasion-specific polemic, the eldership question shifts. The masculine "husband of one wife" qualification in 1 Tim 3 and Titus 1 is written into the same Ephesian situation as 1 Tim 2 — same letter to the same congregation in the same fight against the same opposition. If that opposition included anti-marriage asceticism (1 Tim 4:3) and cult-formed women teaching Eve-priority myths, then requiring elders to be settled married men is doing two things at once: (a) excluding the ascetic stream that opposed marriage, and (b) restoring an ordered household pattern against the chaos the cult was producing. The list is not necessarily a context-free decree that no woman may ever hold the elder office across all centuries. It is what Paul prescribed to Timothy at Ephesus, with reasons partly internal to Ephesus. That said, the broader pattern — the Twelve, the named apostles, the named elders across multiple churches — does have a maleness to it that cannot be reduced to Ephesian polemic alone, and that broader pattern is what this section examines.*

1. **The Twelve** (Mark 3:13–19) — Jesus chose twelve men. The number is symbolic (the twelve tribes), but the maleness is not exegeted as symbolic anywhere in the text. Jesus had female disciples (Luke 8:1–3), funded by women (the same passage), entrusted the resurrection announcement to women (Matt 28; John 20) — and still chose twelve men for the foundational apostolic role.

2. **Elder qualifications** (1 Tim 3:1–7) — "An overseer must be above reproach, *the husband of one wife*, sober-minded, self-controlled..." (literally *mias gunaikos andra*, "a one-woman man"). The phrase could be read as "faithfully married" rather than "male and married," but the entire qualification list uses masculine pronouns and the marital qualification assumes a male subject.

3. **Titus 1:5–9** — "Appoint elders in every town as I directed you — if anyone is above reproach, *the husband of one wife*, and his children are believers..." Same pattern, written to a different congregation in a different region.

4. **Junia** (Rom 16:7) — "Greet Andronicus and Junia, my kinsmen and my fellow prisoners. They are well known to the apostles, and they were in Christ before me." Almost certainly a woman (the name was read as feminine for the first thousand years of church history, and the masculine reading is a late conjecture). "Well known *to* the apostles" vs. "well known *among* the apostles" is contested. Either way, a female figure is named in the apostolic vicinity. She is the cited exception, and exceptions matter, but exceptions are not the rule.

5. **Phoebe** (Rom 16:1–2) — "I commend to you our sister Phoebe, a *diakonos* of the church at Cenchreae... a patron of many and of myself as well." Deacon (the same word used in 1 Tim 3:8) and *prostatis* (patron/benefactor, a word of social standing and oversight). Female deacons are clearly attested. Female elders are not.

6. **Priscilla** (Acts 18:24–26) — She and Aquila take Apollos aside and "explained to him the way of God more accurately." Priscilla teaches a man, privately, with her husband present. The text is not embarrassed. Whatever Paul's restriction in 1 Tim 2 means, it does not mean "women may never explain doctrine to men under any circumstances" — that would contradict Acts 18.

7. **The female prophets and patrons** — Lydia hosts the Philippian church (Acts 16). Nympha hosts a church in her house (Col 4:15). Chloe's people report to Paul (1 Cor 1:11). The four daughters of Philip prophesy (Acts 21:9). Women fund, host, prophesy, evangelize, teach individuals — but the named elders and overseers and apostles are men.

**What survives the cultural lens — revised:** A pattern, but a softer one than the older reading claimed. The Pastorals' male-elder qualifications, read inside the anti-cult polemic of §V, lose much of their force as a universal rule and become significantly more context-bound. What remains:

- A *broader* pattern across multiple churches and decades in which the named elders, overseers, and apostles are predominantly male — Junia and Phoebe as real but bounded counterweights, not as the rule
- A *prophesying, discipling, evangelizing, hosting, patronizing* ministry that is unambiguously open to both sexes — and the apostolic record (Priscilla, Junia, Phoebe, Lydia, Nympha, Chloe, the daughters of Philip) shows women operating across that whole range
- A *Twelve* that remains all-male even though Jesus had every cultural latitude to choose otherwise (he chose female disciples, female resurrection witnesses, female patrons) and is the figure least bound by Ephesian polemic

The honest position: the apostolic pattern *leans* male in the settled governing-teaching office, the Twelve in particular do, and that pattern asks for an account. But "Paul absolutely forbids women elders in 1 Tim 3" is no longer the strongest reading of 1 Tim 3 once the Pastorals' polemic context is taken seriously. The case for a universal male-eldership rule now has to rest on the *broader* pattern (the Twelve, the canonical symbolic grammar of §VIII below, the priesthood-icon argument) rather than on the Pastorals' qualification list read as a free-standing decree. That is a real weakening of the traditional complementarian case, and naming it honestly is part of what reading these texts in their actual first-century environment costs us.

## VII. Pastor, Elder, Overseer: One Office in Three Lenses

*The previous section's argument depends on a claim that may be unfamiliar: the New Testament has no separate "pastor" office above, below, or beside the elders. The English word "pastor" is just the Latin for shepherd, and the only NT text that uses "shepherd" as a leadership title for humans is Ephesians 4:11. Everywhere else, the work of shepherding is named as the work of elders. The modern "Senior Pastor" — visionary CEO of the local church, with elders as a board of advisors — is a 19th–20th century organizational accretion, not a biblical office. Untangling this matters: a large portion of the modern complementarian intuition about women in ministry rests on a polity that does not exist in the NT, and dissolves once the actual biblical structure is recovered.*

1. **Three Greek terms, one office** —
   - ***Presbyteros*** ("elder") — ~70x in NT. Inherits the synagogue *zaqen* tradition: a council of mature, vetted men who governed local Jewish communities. Names the *person*.
   - ***Episkopos*** ("overseer / bishop") — ~5x. Greek civic vocabulary for an administrative supervisor. Names the *function*.
   - ***Poimēn*** ("shepherd / pastor") — ~18x, but almost all literal sheep-keeping or of Jesus as Good Shepherd. Appears as a human leadership title exactly once: Ephesians 4:11. Names the *image*.

2. **The smoking-gun passages — all three terms applied to the same group:**
   - **Acts 20:17, 28** — Paul summons "the **elders** (*presbyteroi*) of the church" at Ephesus. Addresses them: "The Holy Spirit has made you **overseers** (*episkopoi*), to **shepherd** (*poimainein*) the church of God." Same men. Three terms in three verses. One office.
   - **Titus 1:5–7** — "appoint **elders** in every town... For an **overseer**, as God's steward, must be above reproach..." Paul shifts terms mid-sentence describing the same role.
   - **1 Peter 5:1–2** — "I exhort the **elders** among you... **shepherd** the flock of God that is among you, exercising **oversight**." All three concepts, one group, two verses.
   - **1 Tim 3 + 5:17** — chapter 3 lists qualifications for *episkopos*; chapter 5 says "let the *presbyteroi* who rule well be considered worthy of double honor, **especially those who labor in preaching and teaching**." Same office, two terms, with a noted subset who do more teaching.
   - **Phil 1:1** — "to all the saints... with the **overseers** (plural) and **deacons**." Plural overseers. No senior pastor standing over them.

3. **What this means** — elder, overseer, and shepherd are not three offices; they are three lenses on one office. Elder names the *person* (mature, vetted, older in the faith); overseer names the *function* (watching, governing, stewardship); shepherd names the *image* (leading, feeding, protecting the flock). **There is no separate "pastor" class anywhere in the New Testament.**

4. **Ephesians 4:11 — the one exception that proves the rule** — "He gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the *poimenas* and *didaskalous*..." The Greek construction is *tous de poimenas kai didaskalous* — one article governing two nouns joined by *kai*. In koine this typically marks a single combined class or two heavily overlapping ones, not two distinct gifts. The text is best read "the shepherds-and-teachers" — a fused designation. Compare 1 Tim 5:17, which describes the same group: elders, *especially those who labor in preaching and teaching*. Even the one passage where *poimēn* functions as a title puts it inside an elder-teacher fusion, not above it.

5. **Plurality is a structural feature of NT eldership** — Acts 14:23 ("when they had appointed **elders** [plural] for them in every church"); Titus 1:5 ("appoint **elders** in every town"); James 5:14 ("call for the **elders** of the church"); Phil 1:1 (overseers, plural). Every named local church in the NT that has named leadership has *plural* leadership. The solo-pastor model is not the NT model.

6. **First among equals, not above the council** — 1 Tim 5:17 names a subset of elders who labor in preaching and teaching and are honored with "double honor" for it. This is the closest the NT comes to naming a teaching specialist within the eldership. But notice what it does *not* say: it does not place this elder over the others, does not make him the executive officer of the elder council, does not give him casting-vote authority. He is recognized for his particular labor; he is not promoted above the body that called him.

7. **Deacons are a separate office, but not a higher one** — Phil 1:1 distinguishes overseers and deacons as two named offices. 1 Tim 3 lists qualifications for both. The prototype is Acts 6: the Twelve hand off the daily distribution of food to seven Spirit-filled men so they can devote themselves to prayer and the ministry of the word. Deacons serve the practical-mercy-administrative ministry. They are not under-elders or middle managers; they are a distinct vocation of servant leadership. Phoebe holds this office (Rom 16:1), and the NT shows no hesitation about women in it.

8. **How the modern Senior Pastor model got built** — a stack of post-apostolic accretions, each one defensible in its moment but cumulatively producing a polity the NT does not authorize:
   - **Reformation split** (16th c.) — Calvin's Geneva distinguished *teaching elder* (full-time minister of the Word) from *ruling elder* (lay governance). Useful pragmatically; introduced a status differential the NT does not name.
   - **Anglican retention of the bishop** (16th c.) — Henry VIII's break with Rome kept the episcopal hierarchy. The pastor-as-priest model persisted, with bishop above.
   - **Frontier circuit-rider** (18th–19th c.) — Baptist and Methodist preachers served multiple congregations across vast geography. The traveling "preacher" became visibly distinct from the settled local lay leadership.
   - **20th-century professionalization** — seminary-trained, salaried "Senior Pastor" as full-time church staff, with elders as volunteer lay leaders. Power asymmetry built into the org chart.
   - **Mega-church corporate import** (1980s–) — Peter Drucker's management theory ran directly into evangelical polity through Bob Buford's Leadership Network. Willow Creek, Saddleback, and successors explicitly adopted business-org-chart structure: Pastor = CEO, Elders = Board, Deacons = middle management, Staff = employees, Congregation = consumers. Not metaphor. Actual organizational consulting. The vision-casting executive pastor with PowerPoint and personal brand is a 1990s artifact.
   - **American executive instinct** — the inherited cultural assumption that any institution needs a CEO, that single-figure accountability is more efficient than collegial governance, and that the church should be run like a successful organization.

9. ***Ezer kenegdo* at the church level — distributed, not pairwise** — the CEO/Board model creates an *artificial* driver-vs-checker structure: pastor pushes, elders restrain. This looks marriage-shaped because we imported the shape from corporate hierarchy. But the counterweight-against-conquest function is biblically distributed *across* the plural eldership, not concentrated in a board-to-CEO relationship. The elders are each other's counterweights. The teaching elder's drift is checked by his peers; their drift is checked by him. The accountability is *horizontal*, not vertical. This is *less* marriage-shaped than the CEO/Board polity suggested, and *more* apostolic-band-shaped: a group of peers under Christ's headship, each carrying part of the watching-against-drift function. The church is not a household with one head; it is a flock with one Head (Christ) and several under-shepherds.

10. **The "teaching pastor" edge case — face, not head** — the user's question deserves a direct answer. Modern church structures often feature a *teaching pastor* whose explicit role is the pulpit: weekly preaching, public face, exegetical work — but who is *not* the chief executive, sometimes not even an elder. Three configurations need to be distinguished:

    **(a) Lead elder who teaches most** — the biblical pattern of 1 Tim 5:17. He sits on the elder council as one voice among several, but does the bulk of the preaching and is recognized for it. This is just an elder with a teaching specialization. The "can a woman pastor" question, in this configuration, collapses entirely into "can a woman be an elder" — answered in §VI and §VIII below. No new ground.

    **(b) Paid staff teaching pastor *outside* the elder council** — a modern arrangement. The teaching pastor preaches but holds no governing authority; the elders govern. She is the *face* of the pulpit but not the *head* of the church. **This configuration is novel — the NT does not contemplate it directly** — but it maps closely onto the NT's *prophet-teacher* category, which the apostolic record treats as substantially open to women. Philip's four daughters prophesied (Acts 21:9). Anna prophesied in the temple courts (Luke 2:36–38). Women prophesy publicly in 1 Cor 11:5. Priscilla teaches Apollos doctrine (Acts 18:26). Spirit-given speech and instruction is unrestricted in the apostolic record across both sexes. The 1 Tim 2:12 restriction (already occasion-specific per §V) targets *teaching with authority* — *didaskein* paired with *authentein*. Strip the *authentein* — strip the governance — and what is left is *didaskein*, which the NT freely grants to prophets and teachers of both sexes. **A woman serving as teaching pastor (face, not head) in this configuration faces almost no biblical objection that survives §V.**
    
    Honest caveat: the pulpit carries enormous *functional* authority even without governance vote-rights. What is preached weekly shapes the church's theology, ethics, vision, and self-understanding. To pretend "she has no authority, only a microphone" is partly fiction. So the symbolic-grammar argument of §VIII still applies with reduced force — does the church experience her preaching as exercising a fatherly/iconic headship? In most evangelical contexts, probably less so than the lead-pastor role does; the elders govern, and the teaching pastor is heard as a gifted teacher rather than as the figurehead-shepherd of the flock. But the residue is not zero.

    **(c) Itinerant or visiting preacher** — invited from outside, no embedded role. This is clearly open. The Priscilla model (Acts 18) and the prophetic-women pattern make it unambiguous. Most evangelical churches that forbid women from the local pulpit will happily host a woman missionary, evangelist, or visiting Bible teacher to preach in chapel or at conferences — implicitly conceding the principle.

11. **What the eldership is, what it is not** — to crystallize:
    - **The elder is** a Spirit-set, congregation-recognized overseer who watches over the flock, governs collegially with fellow elders, teaches the faith, guards the doctrine, models the life, and bears stewardship of the local body
    - **The elder is not** a board member sitting above an executive pastor
    - **The pastor is** an elder who shepherds and teaches — the same office under a different lens
    - **The pastor is not** a CEO above a board
    - **The teaching pastor** (in the modern paid-staff sense) is closest to a *recognized prophet-teacher* embedded in a particular congregation. The NT does not have this exact role, but it has a near-cousin, and that near-cousin is substantially sex-open
    - **The congregation is** the flock, the body, the bride — under elders, under Christ
    - **The congregation is not** consumers of a Sunday-morning product, however the modern model has trained them to behave

**What survives the cultural lens — and what does not:**

The CEO/Board model was secretly carrying a large part of the complementarian intuition about female pastors. Once it dissolves into the actual biblical collegial eldership, three things follow:

- The "woman pastor" question is not a single question; it splits into the three configurations above, with different answers
- For configuration (b) — face but not head — the case for prohibition is *very* thin, and depends almost entirely on a residual symbolic-grammar argument that does not bear universal-prohibition weight
- For configuration (a) — lead elder who teaches — the question reduces to the women-elder question, on which §V Interlude has already shifted the ground significantly

A church can adopt a polity in which women serve as teaching pastors (face, not head) while reserving the eldership for men, and this is *internally coherent* with everything this handout has identified as residue. It is not the only coherent position — full eldership openness is also defensible — but it is the configuration that most carefully honors both the apostolic-pattern pull on the eldership and the apostolic-record openness on the teaching-prophet-evangelist gift. Many traditions already do something like this in practice without naming it clearly.

What does *not* survive: any claim that "the Bible forbids women pastors" if "pastor" means *teaching specialist*. The Bible does not forbid that. What the Bible patterns is *plural male eldership with one or more elders bearing primary teaching responsibility*, alongside *open prophetic and teaching ministry by women in apostolic-record practice*. The collision the modern church experiences is largely a collision between an unbiblical polity (Senior Pastor as CEO) and a partial reading of the apostolic record.

## VIII. Symbolic and Liturgical Asymmetry

*The deepest residue is not in any single passage but in the consistent symbolic grammar of the whole canon. God is named in masculine terms — Father, Son, King, Husband, Bridegroom, Shepherd, Warrior — even where feminine images are borrowed. The church is consistently named as bride. Israel is consistently named as wife. The Incarnation is male. The eschatological marriage supper is of the Lamb and His bride. These are not interchangeable. The symbolic asymmetry of the Bible is so pervasive that any theology which tries to flatten it ends up redacting half the text.*

1. **God as Father** — The first-person address Jesus teaches is "Our Father." Mother-imagery appears for God (Isa 49:15; 66:13; Matt 23:37) but God is never *named* Mother. The grammar of address is consistent across both testaments.

2. **God as Husband, Israel as Wife** — Hosea's marriage is the controlling metaphor for the covenant. Isaiah 54:5: "Your Maker is your husband." Jeremiah 31:32: "I was their husband." Ezekiel 16, 23: the betrayed-husband narrative drives entire chapters of prophetic theology.

3. **Christ as Bridegroom, Church as Bride** — John the Baptist (John 3:29), Jesus Himself (Matt 9:15, 25:1–13), Paul (2 Cor 11:2; Eph 5:25–32), Revelation (19:7–9; 21:2, 9; 22:17). The marriage-supper-of-the-Lamb is the climactic image of the consummation, and the gendered roles are not incidental.

4. **The Incarnation is male** — God could have become incarnate as a woman. He did not. This is a fact of revelation, and Christian theology has historically taken it seriously rather than dismissing it as a cultural accident. The maleness of Jesus is not soteriologically incidental; it sits inside the Father-Son grammar that the New Testament uses to describe the inner life of God.

5. **The priesthood was male** — The Levitical priesthood, the apostolic foundation, the elder-overseer office: all male. Women served at the tabernacle (Ex 38:8; 1 Sam 2:22) and the temple (Luke 2:37), but the sacrificial-mediatorial role was male. The pattern continues into the apostolic office and the post-apostolic eldership.

6. **The female counterweight** — Mary, not Peter, is the first to receive the news of the Incarnation. Women, not the apostles, are the first witnesses of the resurrection. The proto-evangelium (Gen 3:15) names the *seed of the woman*, not the seed of the man, as the one who will crush the serpent. Wisdom (Prov 8) is personified as a woman. The church-as-bride is the eschatological identity of every believer, male and female. The female pole is honored at every climactic moment of the salvation story — but as the *receiving, bearing, trusting* pole, not the initiating-mediating one. The same *ezer kenegdo* logic of Genesis 2 runs through the whole canon: the male initiating-conquering motion is consistently met, balanced, and corrected by a female receiving-bearing-opposing motion. Israel as the wife who answers YHWH; Mary as the daughter of Zion whose *yes* opposes Eve's *no*; the church as the bride whose trust completes the Bridegroom's self-giving. Strip the female pole out and the salvation story loses its counterweight.

**What survives the cultural lens:** A consistent symbolic grammar in which God, Christ, the Father, the Bridegroom, the Priest, the King are male, and the church, Israel, the bride, the wisdom-receiver are female. The roles are *not* interchangeable in the text. A theology that insists they should be ends up rewriting the imagery the Bible actually uses, which is a high price to pay for symmetry. **This is the residue that does not soften under the §V Interlude.** Unlike 1 Tim 2 or 1 Tim 3, the symbolic grammar is not Ephesian-context-bound — it runs across both testaments, from Genesis to Revelation, through prophets and gospels and apostolic letters. Whatever Paul was correcting in Ephesus, he did not invent the bride-of-YHWH metaphor or the Father-Son grammar; those are canonical bedrock. If the universal asymmetry between men and women lives anywhere in the text, it lives here.

===
## IX. Synthesis: Equal-but-Different, Precisely Defined

The whole picture compresses into a single line:

> **A wife should submit to a husband who would die for her — and in that submission, expose the desires of his that are not focused on the Kingdom (that is, on everyone else).**

Both halves are load-bearing. *Submit* — because the Christ-church icon requires the asymmetric posture. *To a husband who would die for her* — because the only headship Scripture authorizes is the cruciform kind, and a husband unwilling to die for her has forfeited the role he is asking her to honor. *Expose the desires not focused on the Kingdom* — because that is what *ezer kenegdo* actually does: the counterweight makes visible the conquest-drift, the self-interest, the small ambitions that creep in whenever male initiative is left unopposed. Submission without exposure becomes silent complicity. Exposure without submission becomes rivalry. The biblical marriage is the union of the two: a trusting posture from which the wife names what the husband cannot see in himself, oriented by the Kingdom and the good of everyone else.

After all of this, what can be said with precision? The egalitarian wins are real and should not be surrendered. The complementarian instincts are also real and should not be dismissed. The settled biblical picture is neither of the two slogans the modern church usually fights about — *not* a flat interchangeability of sexes in every role, *not* a hierarchy of value in which women are spiritually or intellectually subordinate. It is a third thing, harder to slogan-ize but more faithful to the text.

**In the family:**

- **Equal in image, dignity, and inheritance.** Both bear God's image. Both are co-heirs of grace (1 Pet 3:7; Gal 3:28). Neither is closer to God by virtue of sex. The marriage is between two whole persons.
- **Asymmetric in headship and submission.** The husband bears a *self-giving, sacrificial* headship modeled on Christ's death for the church — not a tyrannical command authority. The wife bears a *trusting, responsive* submission modeled on the church's posture toward Christ — not silence or self-erasure. Both vocations are difficult; the husband's is, on Paul's terms, the harder one.
- **Asymmetric as counterweight — ezer kenegdo.** The wife is the *strength that stands opposite*. Her role is not to second the husband's motions but to balance them: to oppose his conquest-drift with relational pull, to resist where resistance is what love requires, to refuse to dissolve into either silent compliance or rivalrous combat. Submission and opposition are not contradictions; they are the two sides of how a counterweight functions inside a trusted partnership. A marriage in which the wife has no opposing voice has lost the very feature God built her to provide.
- **Asymmetric in childbearing and primary nurture.** The maternal vocation is real, theologically dense, and uniquely female. The paternal vocation is real, theologically dense, and uniquely male. Modern marriages can negotiate practical labor in countless ways, but the underlying biological and theological asymmetry remains the ground from which the negotiation begins.
- **One-flesh union as icon.** The marriage is a living picture of Christ and the church. The picture requires distinct roles. Interchangeability destroys the icon.

**In ministry — with §VII's pastor/elder distinction in hand:**

- **All gifts available to both sexes.** Prophecy, teaching, evangelism, mercy, administration, hospitality, healing, tongues, interpretation, faith, giving, leadership, exhortation — all distributed by the Spirit as He wills (1 Cor 12:11), without sex as a filter.
- **Prophetic and Spirit-given speech is unrestricted.** Women prophesy in both Testaments. Paul assumes this in 1 Cor 11. Any practice that silences women's prophetic voice contradicts both Joel 2 and Acts 2.
- **Discipleship, teaching, evangelism, and the full range of ministry are unrestricted.** Priscilla teaches Apollos. Phoebe is a *diakonos* and *prostatis*. Junia is named among or alongside the apostles. Older women teach younger women. Mothers and grandmothers teach Timothy. Women host churches, fund the mission, evangelize, catechize, write, preach evangelistically, prophesy. None of this is in tension with Paul.
- **The diaconate is open.** Phoebe in Rom 16:1 is *diakonos* of the church at Cenchreae. 1 Tim 3:11 most likely addresses female deacons. The diaconate is not under-eldership; it is its own vocation, and the apostolic record shows no hesitation about women in it.
- **The Pastorals' specific restrictions are occasion-specific, not universal.** 1 Tim 2:11–15 and the male framing of 1 Tim 3 / Titus 1 are written into the Ephesian cult-polemic situation (§V). They cannot be lifted out and applied as universal decrees. This is a real correction to older complementarian readings, and it costs us a chunk of what those readings claimed to know.
- **"Pastor" is not a separate office from "elder."** The modern Senior Pastor / CEO model is a 19th–20th c. organizational accretion, not a biblical office (§VII). Once dissolved into actual biblical collegial eldership, the "can a woman pastor" question splits into three configurations, each answered differently.
- **Teaching pastor as *face but not head* (§VII.10b) is substantially open.** A woman who preaches but does not govern — face of the pulpit, not head of the church — maps closely onto the NT prophet-teacher role, which the apostolic record treats as substantially sex-open. The 1 Tim 2 restriction targets *teaching with governing authority*; strip the governance and the restriction loses most of its grip. A church that places women in teaching-pastor roles while reserving the elder council for men is internally coherent with everything this handout has identified as residue.
- **Itinerant or visiting teacher / preacher (§VII.10c) is unambiguously open.** The Priscilla model and the prophetic-women pattern decide this. Most evangelical churches concede it implicitly by welcoming women missionaries, evangelists, and conference teachers.
- **Lead elder who teaches (§VII.10a) is the contested case.** Here the residual asymmetry — symbolic-grammar argument, broader apostolic pattern, the Twelve — actually applies. A church can defend an all-male eldership on these grounds, but it is a *patterned* defense, not a *prohibition*, and the §V Interlude shows the case is weaker than older complementarian readings made it.
- **The symbolic grammar of Father / Bridegroom / Christ / male Incarnation is the residue that does not soften.** Wherever the universal "men and women are not interchangeable" claim lives in the text, it lives in the canonical imagery of §VIII, not in the Pastorals' occasion-specific rules — and it most clearly bears on the *iconic-headship* role (the elder who stands as fatherly steward of the congregation), not on the teaching role per se.

**The shape of the disagreement, revised:**

- The hard egalitarian must still account for the *pattern* of male apostleship/eldership, the *embodied difference* of childbearing, and the *symbolic grammar* of Christ-as-Bridegroom and church-as-bride. None of these dissolves into Ephesian polemic; each remains in the text after the §V Interlude is granted.
- The hard complementarian must surrender (i) the easiest proof-text move — lifting 1 Tim 2:12 as a universal decree — once the Pastorals' anti-cult context is taken seriously (§V); and (ii) the assumption that the Senior Pastor / CEO model is biblical (§VII). The remaining case for a sex-specific governing office has to rest on the Twelve, the broader apostolic pattern, and the symbolic grammar — a real case, but a weaker and more contested one than 1 Tim 2 + 1 Tim 3 in isolation appeared to provide.

The position this handout commends sits between: **broad equality of gifts and ministries, narrow asymmetry in marriage and in the canonical symbolic grammar, with the question of women elders as an open question that the broader apostolic pattern leans into but cannot fully decide.** Husband as self-giving head; wife as trusting partner and *ezer kenegdo* counterweight. Both sexes as prophets, teachers, evangelists, deacons, teaching-pastors-without-governance, apostolic-adjacent figures, hosts, patrons, disciplers, image-bearers, co-heirs. The headship of the marriage retains a sex-specific shape — grounded in creation, sealed by the Christ-church icon, and lived as cruciform self-giving on the husband's side and trust-with-exposure on the wife's. The lead-elder office, where the iconic-headship residue is sharpest, is *less settled* than older readings claimed; the teaching-pastor role, where iconic headship is *not* the operative function, is substantially open. Honesty requires distinguishing these.
