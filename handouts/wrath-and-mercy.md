# The Wrath of God: Atrocity or Redemption?

How do we handle the seemingly wrathful God of the Torah?

One resolution to the trajectory from wrath to mercy would be to say that God has learned how to be a better God over time — but even the staunchest Open Theist would have trouble backing that statement. While the complete immutability of God might be more Plato than Bible, God has always been good, and has always been love.

As early as Joshua and Judges, much of what we call "wrath" can be explained as God allowing Israel to receive the natural consequences of their disobedience — for the ultimate purpose of redeeming the remnant.

---

# The "Atrocities" by Category

Each category represents a different *shape* of moral objection — and will likely require a different shape of answer.

---

## Category A: God Directly Destroys

*God personally inflicts death or destruction on a massive scale. No human intermediary — fire, flood, plague, earthquake.*

1. **The Flood** (Gen 6–8) — All life on earth destroyed except one family and the ark's cargo.
2. **Sodom and Gomorrah** (Gen 19) — Fire from heaven incinerates entire cities, including children and infants.
3. **The Ten Plagues of Egypt** (Ex 7–12) — Escalating devastation culminating in the death of every firstborn son in Egypt.
4. **Fire on Nadab and Abihu** (Lev 10:1–2) — Aaron's sons offer "unauthorized fire" and are consumed by fire from the LORD's presence.
5. **The Earth Swallows Korah's Household** (Num 16:31–35) — The ground opens and swallows entire families alive; fire consumes 250 men offering incense.
6. **Plague After Korah's Rebellion** (Num 16:46–50) — 14,700 die in a plague the *next day* for grumbling about Korah's punishment.
7. **Serpents in the Wilderness** (Num 21:4–9) — Venomous snakes sent among Israel for complaining about food and water.
8. **Plague at Baal Peor** (Num 25) — 24,000 die in a plague for intermarrying with Moabites and worshipping their gods.
9. **Plague After David's Census** (2 Sam 24) — 70,000 die because David counted his fighting men. God *incited* the census (v. 1), then punished it.
10. **Destruction of Sennacherib's Army** (2 Kings 19:35) — The angel of the LORD kills 185,000 Assyrian soldiers in a single night.

**Honorable mentions:** Fire at Taberah (Num 11:1–3); plague after the quail/manna complaints (Num 11:33–34); death of the 10 faithless spies (Num 14:36–37).

## Category B: God Commands Israel to Kill

*God orders human agents to carry out violence — including against women, children, and livestock. The moral weight falls on the command, not just the act.*

1. **The Canaanite Conquest / Herem** (Deut 7:1–2; 20:16–18; Josh 6–11) — "You shall devote them to complete destruction... show no mercy." Entire populations commanded to be wiped out.
2. **The Amalekite Genocide** (1 Sam 15:3) — "Kill both man and woman, child and infant, ox and sheep, camel and donkey."
3. **The Midianite War and Taking of Virgins** (Num 31) — After battle, Moses is angry that the women were spared; commands killing all non-virgin women and boys. 32,000 virgin girls kept alive.
4. **Levites Kill 3,000 After the Golden Calf** (Ex 32:27–28) — "Each of you kill his brother and his companion and his neighbor." The Levites earn their priestly role through this slaughter.
5. **Execution of Achan's Family** (Josh 7:24–26) — Achan stole from Jericho's spoil. His sons, daughters, oxen, donkeys, sheep, tent, and everything he had were stoned and burned.
6. **Phinehas Executes the Couple at Baal Peor** (Num 25:6–13) — Drives a spear through an Israelite man and Midianite woman mid-act. God rewards him with "a covenant of peace" and permanent priesthood.
7. **Destruction of Jericho** (Josh 6:21) — "They devoted the city to the LORD and destroyed with the sword every living thing in it — men and women, young and old, cattle, sheep and donkeys."
8. **The Slaughter of the Five Amorite Kings** (Josh 10:22–27) — Joshua has the kings brought out of the cave, humiliated, executed, and hung on trees until evening.
9. **Samuel Hacks Agag to Pieces** (1 Sam 15:33) — After Saul disobediently spares the Amalekite king, Samuel personally butchers him "before the LORD."
10. **Elijah Slaughters the Prophets of Baal** (1 Kings 18:40) — After the Mount Carmel contest, Elijah has 450 prophets seized and killed at the Kishon Valley.

**Honorable mentions:** The ban on Ai (Josh 8:24–29); seven nations of Canaan (Deut 7:1); destruction of Hazor (Josh 11:10–11).

## Category C: God "Overreacts" to Holiness Violations

*A seemingly minor offense meets lethal or devastating punishment. The punishment appears wildly disproportionate to the crime.*

1. **Uzzah Steadies the Ark** (2 Sam 6:6–7) — Reaches out to keep the Ark from falling. Struck dead instantly. David is angry *at God*.
2. **The Bears and the Youths** (2 Kings 2:23–24) — Young people mock Elisha's baldness. Two bears maul 42 of them.
3. **Nadab and Abihu's "Strange Fire"** (Lev 10:1–2) — Aaron's newly ordained sons offer incense in a way not prescribed. Consumed by fire.
4. **Lot's Wife Looks Back** (Gen 19:26) — Turns to look at the burning city. Becomes a pillar of salt.
5. **Er, Judah's Firstborn** (Gen 38:7) — "Er was wicked in the sight of the LORD, and the LORD put him to death." No further explanation.
6. **Onan** (Gen 38:8–10) — Refuses to fulfill levirate duty by "spilling his seed." God kills him too.
7. **Moses Banned from the Promised Land** (Num 20:7–12) — After 40 years of faithful leadership, Moses strikes a rock instead of speaking to it. Barred from entering the land.
8. **The Man Gathering Sticks on the Sabbath** (Num 15:32–36) — Found picking up sticks on Saturday. God commands the entire community to stone him to death.
9. **Miriam Struck with Leprosy** (Num 12:1–15) — Criticizes Moses' marriage. Gets leprosy for seven days. Aaron, who also criticized, gets nothing.
10. **Ananias and Sapphira** (Acts 5:1–11) — Lie about a real estate donation to the early church. Both drop dead on the spot. (New Testament, but same pattern.)

**Honorable mentions:** The Bethshemesh men who looked into the Ark — 70 killed (1 Sam 6:19); Uzziah struck with leprosy for burning incense (2 Chron 26:16–21).

## Category D: God Manipulates or Removes Human Agency

*God appears to override free will, set up no-win scenarios, or orchestrate suffering as a "test" — raising questions about fairness, consent, and moral responsibility.*

1. **Hardening Pharaoh's Heart** (Ex 4–14) — God removes Pharaoh's capacity to repent, then punishes him and his nation for not repenting.
2. **The Binding of Isaac** (Gen 22) — God commands Abraham to sacrifice his son as a test. Psychological trauma for both, with a last-second intervention.
3. **God Sends a Lying Spirit** (1 Kings 22:19–23) — God authorizes a spirit to deceive King Ahab's prophets so Ahab will go to battle and die.
4. **God Hardens Israel's Heart** (Isa 6:9–10) — "Make the heart of this people dull... lest they turn and be healed." God commissions Isaiah to *prevent* repentance.
5. **Job's Suffering as a Wager** (Job 1–2) — God allows Satan to destroy a righteous man's family, health, and livelihood to settle a challenge about the nature of faith.
6. **God Incites David to Take the Census** (2 Sam 24:1) — God moves David to count Israel, then punishes him for it. (1 Chron 21:1 attributes it to Satan — the texts contradict.)
7. **Jacob and Esau: Loved and Hated Before Birth** (Mal 1:2–3; Rom 9:10–13) — "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" — before either had done anything good or bad.
8. **God Sends Strong Delusion** (2 Thess 2:11) — "God sends them a strong delusion, so that they may believe what is false" — active divine deception of those who rejected truth.
9. **God Gives People Over to Their Desires** (Rom 1:24–28) — Three times: "God gave them up" — not merely allowing sin but actively handing people over to it.
10. **Judas' Predetermined Betrayal** (John 13:18, 27; Acts 1:16) — "The Scripture had to be fulfilled." Jesus identifies Judas as chosen *for* this purpose. "Satan entered into him" — after Jesus handed him the bread.

**Honorable mentions:** God deceiving prophets (Ezek 14:9); Shimei cursing David "because the LORD told him to" (2 Sam 16:10); Samson's marriage to a Philistine woman as God's initiative (Judg 14:4).

## Category E: God Punishes Many for the Sins of Few

*Collective punishment — families, communities, or entire nations suffer for the actions of one person or a small group.*

1. **Achan's Family** (Josh 7:24–26) — One man hides plunder from Jericho. His sons, daughters, and animals are stoned and burned.
2. **Korah's Household** (Num 16) — Korah, Dathan, and Abiram rebel. The earth swallows their entire families — wives, children, possessions.
3. **David's Census Plague** (2 Sam 24) — David sins by counting the people. 70,000 of *the people* die.
4. **The Flood** (Gen 6–8) — All humanity destroyed for the wickedness of "every intention of the thoughts of his heart."
5. **Egypt's Firstborn** (Ex 12) — Every household in Egypt loses a child for Pharaoh's refusal.
6. **Curse on Canaan for Ham's Sin** (Gen 9:20–27) — Ham sees Noah's nakedness. Noah curses Ham's *son* Canaan and his descendants.
7. **Generational Curses** (Ex 20:5; 34:7) — "Visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation."
8. **Famine for Saul's Sin Against the Gibeonites** (2 Sam 21:1–9) — Three years of famine on all Israel for a broken treaty. Resolved by executing seven of Saul's descendants.
9. **The Exile** (2 Kings 17, 25) — Entire nations deported — farmers, children, the faithful remnant — for the sins of kings and the ruling class.
10. **Sodom's Destruction** (Gen 18–19) — Abraham bargains God down to ten righteous people. Apparently fewer than ten existed — but their children?

**Honorable mentions:** Original sin / the Fall affecting all humanity (Gen 3; Rom 5:12); Eli's household cursed for his sons' behavior (1 Sam 2–4); Israel punished for Jeroboam's golden calves for centuries.

## Category F: God Endorses or Regulates What Seems Evil

*God doesn't just tolerate these practices — He legislates them. The Law of Moses provides rules for institutions modern readers find morally repugnant.*

1. **Chattel Slavery** (Ex 21:1–11; Lev 25:44–46) — Foreign slaves are "property" that can be inherited. Hebrew slaves serve six years, but a loophole (Ex 21:4–6) creates permanent bondage through family attachment.
2. **War Brides** (Deut 21:10–14) — An Israelite soldier may take a captive woman as a wife. She shaves her head, mourns one month, then he "goes in to her."
3. **Forced Marriage of Rapist to Victim** (Deut 22:28–29) — If a man rapes an unbetrothed virgin, he pays the father 50 shekels and must marry her. She has no recorded say.
4. **Polygamy** — Regulated, never condemned. Abraham, Jacob, David, Solomon all practice it under apparent divine tolerance. Deut 21:15–17 legislates inheritance rights for children of multiple wives.
5. **The Sotah Ordeal** (Num 5:11–31) — A husband suspects his wife of adultery. She drinks "bitter water" in a priestly ritual. If guilty, her "thigh wastes away and her belly swells." No equivalent test for men.
6. **Virginity Test and Execution** (Deut 22:13–21) — If a bride is accused of not being a virgin and her parents cannot produce "evidence of virginity" (the bloodied cloth), she is stoned at her father's door.
7. **Women as Property in Marriage** (Ex 20:17; 22:16–17) — The tenth commandment lists a wife alongside house, servants, ox, and donkey. Bride price paid to the father. Divorce available only to men (Deut 24:1).
8. **Beating Slaves** (Ex 21:20–21) — If a master beats a slave and the slave dies immediately, the master is punished. But if the slave survives "a day or two," no punishment — "for the slave is his money."
9. **Blood Vengeance as Normative** (Num 35; Deut 19) — The "avenger of blood" (a victim's relative) has a legitimate right to kill. Cities of refuge exist not to abolish vengeance but to regulate it.
10. **Herem / Devoted Things** (Lev 27:28–29) — "No one devoted to destruction may be ransomed; they shall surely be put to death." The legal framework for human sacrifice to YHWH by another name.

**Honorable mentions:** Levirate marriage obligations (Deut 25:5–10); daughters sold into servitude (Ex 21:7–11); the institution of animal sacrifice as worship.

## Category G: God Uses Nations as Instruments, Then Punishes Them

*God raises up foreign empires to execute judgment on Israel, then destroys those same empires for the very violence He directed them to commit. A seeming double bind.*

1. **Assyria: "Rod of My Anger"** (Isa 10:5–19) — God sends Assyria to punish Israel. Then declares: "When the Lord has finished all his work on Mount Zion, I will punish the speech of the arrogant heart of the king of Assyria."
2. **Babylon: God's "Servant" Nebuchadnezzar** (Jer 25:9; 27:6) — God explicitly calls Nebuchadnezzar "My servant" and gives him dominion. Then devotes 50 chapters of prophecy to Babylon's destruction.
3. **Pharaoh: "Raised Up for This Purpose"** (Ex 9:16; Rom 9:17) — "For this very purpose I have raised you up, that I might show My power in you." Pharaoh is created to be an object lesson, then destroyed for the role he was made to play.
4. **The Judges-Era Cycle** (Judg 2:14–16) — God "sells" Israel into the hands of oppressors, then raises up judges to deliver them from those same oppressors. The pattern repeats for 300+ years.
5. **Moab and Ammon as Instruments** (2 Kings 24:2) — "The LORD sent against [Jehoiakim] bands of the Chaldeans and bands of the Moabites and bands of the Ammonites." All later face their own prophetic judgment.
6. **Persia: Cyrus as God's "Anointed"** (Isa 44:28–45:7) — God calls Cyrus His "messiah" and "shepherd" — a pagan king who doesn't know YHWH — to accomplish divine purposes. Not punished, but instrumentalized without consent.
7. **Rome in Prophetic Perspective** (Luke 21:20–24; Dan 9:26) — Jesus describes Jerusalem's destruction by Rome as divine judgment: "Days of vengeance, to fulfill all that is written." Rome is God's instrument of covenant judgment.
8. **The Philistines as Chastisement** (1 Sam 4–6) — God allows the Ark to be captured and Israel to be defeated. Then plagues the Philistines for possessing the Ark they were allowed to take.
9. **Syria/Aram** (2 Kings 13:3) — "The anger of the LORD was kindled against Israel, and he gave them continually into the hand of Hazael king of Syria." Aram later faces judgment from Assyria.
10. **Egypt as Broken Reed** (Isa 36:6; Ezek 29–32) — Egypt repeatedly used by God in Israel's story, repeatedly condemned. Called a "broken reed of a staff, which will pierce the hand of any man who leans on it."

**Honorable mentions:** The "four smiths" of Zechariah 1:18–21 (each empire punishes the last); Habakkuk's protest that God uses wicked Babylon to judge less-wicked Israel (Hab 1:13).

# How Christians Answer: Seven Major Viewpoints

## 1. Divine Command Theory

*Classical Reformed / Calvinist*

God is the source of morality itself. Whatever He commands is good *by definition*. Human moral intuition is fallen and unreliable — we don't get to judge God. The Canaanites practiced child sacrifice and extreme depravity; their destruction was just. God has sovereign rights over life and death as Creator.

**Strengths:** Takes the text at face value. Preserves divine sovereignty.

**Weaknesses:** Can feel like "might makes right." Struggles with infant killing.

## 2. Greater Good Theodicy

*Broadly Evangelical*

God sees the entire timeline. These acts prevented greater evil. Canaanite religion — Molech worship, ritual prostitution — would have corrupted Israel and derailed the messianic line. The Flood reset a world so corrupt it was beyond recovery. Harsh measures were surgical, protecting the redemptive plan for *all* humanity.

**Strengths:** Makes moral sense to modern ears. Preserves God's goodness.

**Weaknesses:** Utilitarian logic can justify almost anything. "The ends justify the means" is a dangerous principle.

## 3. Accommodation / Progressive Revelation

*Wesleyan, Moderate Evangelical*

God met Israel where they were in an Ancient Near East context. ANE warfare was universally brutal; God worked *within* that culture to gradually move Israel toward mercy. The herem commands are actually *less* brutal than standard ANE practice — Israel had restrictions other nations didn't.

The trajectory is what matters: Torah, then Prophets, then Jesus, then "love your enemies." God didn't command His ideal; He commanded what a Bronze Age people could receive.

**Strengths:** Explains the trajectory honestly. Honors both the text and Jesus.

**Weaknesses:** Implies God authorized evil, even temporarily. Where exactly is the line?

## 4. Christological Hermeneutic

*Anabaptist, Greg Boyd, some Charismatic*

Jesus is the perfect revelation of God. Anything in the Old Testament that contradicts Jesus' character must be reinterpreted. The violent portraits are Israel *projecting* their ANE assumptions onto God. God "bore" the misattribution the way Christ bore sin on the cross — He allowed Himself to be blamed.

Boyd's "cruciform hermeneutic" reads every OT text through the lens of the cross.

**Strengths:** Radically Christocentric. Takes "love your enemies" as the unchanging character of God.

**Weaknesses:** Effectively overrides large portions of the OT. Borders on Marcionism. Who decides what's "projection"?

## 5. Literary / Genre-Sensitive Reading

*Academic Evangelical, Catholic Scholarship*

Ancient Near Eastern conquest accounts used hyperbolic rhetoric — "utterly destroy" was stock military language, not literal reporting. Archaeological evidence shows that Jericho, Ai, and other "destroyed" cities had continuous habitation. The text itself contradicts total genocide: Canaanites keep showing up *after* Joshua supposedly destroyed them all.

These are theological narratives making a point about loyalty to YHWH, not war journalism.

**Strengths:** Historically and archaeologically grounded. Resolves many internal contradictions.

**Weaknesses:** Can feel like explaining away the text. Doesn't fully address the commands themselves.

## 6. Spiritual Warfare Worldview

*Pentecostal, Charismatic, some Orthodox*

The real war was spiritual. Canaanite territories were under demonic dominion — connected to the Nephilim traditions and the divine council theology of Deuteronomy 32:8. Physical destruction was the visible dimension of cosmic spiritual conflict. God wasn't being cruel to humans; He was reclaiming territory from hostile spiritual powers.

The "giants in the land" tradition connects the Flood, the Canaanites, and the language of total destruction.

**Strengths:** Takes the spiritual worldview of the text seriously. Explains the "total" nature of herem.

**Weaknesses:** Can spiritualize away real human suffering. Some connections are speculative.

## 7. Natural Consequences / Relational Reading

*Open Theism, some Progressive Evangelical*

Much of what Scripture attributes to God's "wrath" is God *withdrawing protection* and allowing consequences. "God hardened Pharaoh's heart" means God's presence *itself* hardened an already resistant heart — the same sun melts wax and hardens clay.

The Flood, Sodom, and similar events were tipping points where evil became self-destructive and God stopped intervening. The cycle of Judges — sin, oppression, crying out, deliverance — is consequences, not punishment.

**Strengths:** Preserves God as love. Aligns deeply with prophetic literature (Hosea, Jeremiah).

**Weaknesses:** Doesn't fully account for passages where God explicitly *commands* violence.
