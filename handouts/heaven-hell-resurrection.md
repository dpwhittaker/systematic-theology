# Heaven, Hell, and Resurrection: Where Are We Going?

A study of what the Bible actually says about the afterlife — examining every major passage about heaven, hell, resurrection, and new earth, and asking whether the popular Christian view of "dying and going to heaven" reflects the biblical hope or a Greek philosophical import.

---

## Guardrails: What All Christians Affirm

Before wading into the internal debates, here are the non-negotiable outer boundaries:

**1. Death is not the end.**
Something happens after we die. The materialist claim that death is simply extinction is outside Christian faith. "It is appointed for man to die once, and after that comes judgment" (Hebrews 9:27, ESV).

**2. Jesus bodily rose from the dead — and His resurrection is the pattern for ours.**
The tomb was empty. The body was gone. He ate fish. He was touched. And Paul says His resurrection is the "firstfruits" of ours (1 Corinthians 15:20). If Jesus' resurrection was physical, ours will be too.

**3. There is a final judgment.**
All humanity will give account. "We must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ" (2 Corinthians 5:10). Justice will be done — fully, finally, and by the One who died for sinners.

**4. God wins.**
Evil does not endure forever. Death is conquered. "The last enemy to be destroyed is death" (1 Corinthians 15:26). Whatever we believe about the details, the story ends with God's victory and His people's vindication.

**5. The redeemed will be with God forever.**
Whether in heaven, on a renewed earth, or in some reality we can't yet imagine — the promise is presence. "So we will always be with the Lord" (1 Thessalonians 4:17).

---

## I. The Question We Rarely Ask

Most Christians, if asked, "What happens when you die?" will say something like: *Your soul goes to heaven to be with God forever.* Some might add: *Or to hell, if you reject Him.*

But is that what the Bible actually teaches?

The answer is more surprising than most people expect. The Bible has remarkably little to say about "going to heaven" as a final destination — and an enormous amount to say about resurrection, new creation, and God coming *down* to dwell with us on a renewed earth.

This handout lays out every major passage and lets the text speak for itself.

---

## II. What the Bible Says About Heaven

The word "heaven" (*shamayim* in Hebrew, *ouranos* in Greek) appears hundreds of times in Scripture. But the vast majority of those uses refer to the *sky* (where birds fly), the *cosmos* (where stars are), or *God's dwelling place* (where His throne is). Very few describe heaven as the place where dead believers go to live forever.

Here is every significant passage used to support the idea that believers "go to heaven":

### The Intermediate State — Between Death and Resurrection

These passages describe what happens *between* death and resurrection. They are temporary, not final.

> Today you will be with me in Paradise. (**Luke 23:43**, ESV)

Jesus says "today" — but three days later He told Mary, "I have not yet ascended to the Father" (John 20:17). Whatever "Paradise" is, it isn't the Father's throne room. And Jesus didn't stay there — He rose bodily.

> For we know that if the tent that is our earthly home is destroyed, we have a building from God, a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens. For in this tent we groan, longing to put on our heavenly dwelling, if indeed by putting it on we may not be found naked. For while we are still in this tent, we groan, being burdened—not that we would be unclothed, but that we would be further clothed, so that what is mortal may be swallowed up by life. He who has prepared us for this very thing is God, who has given us the Spirit as a guarantee. So we are always of good courage. We know that while we are at home in the body we are away from the Lord, for we walk by faith, not by sight. Yes, we are of good courage, and we would rather be away from the body and at home with the Lord. (**2 Corinthians 5:1-8**, ESV)

Paul prefers being "with the Lord" to bodily life — but he calls this disembodied state being "naked" and "unclothed." He doesn't *want* to be disembodied. He wants to be "further clothed, so that what is mortal may be swallowed up by life." The hope is not escaping the body but receiving a *better* body.

> My desire is to depart and be with Christ, for that is far better. (**Philippians 1:23**, ESV)

"With Christ" — wherever He is. This is the intermediate state. Paul knows it's better than suffering in prison. But he also knows the *real* hope is resurrection (Philippians 3:20-21).

> I saw under the altar the souls of those who had been slain for the word of God and for the witness they had borne. They cried out with a loud voice, "O Sovereign Lord, holy and true, how long before you will judge and avenge our blood on those who dwell on the earth?" Then they were each given a white robe and told to rest a little longer, until the number of their fellow servants and their brothers should be complete, who were to be killed as they had been. (**Revelation 6:9-11**, ESV)

Martyrs in heaven — but they're *waiting*. They cry "How long?" They are told to rest. This is not the final destination. They are in the queue for resurrection.

### Things "Stored" in Heaven — Coming Down to Us

These passages describe heaven as a *vault* where God keeps our future blessings safe — not as the place where we go to collect them.

> An inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you, who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time. (**1 Peter 1:4-5**, ESV)

*Kept* in heaven — stored there for safekeeping. And "ready to be revealed in the last time" — it comes to us at the end. Like a gift in a safe, waiting to be delivered.

> The hope laid up for you in heaven. (**Colossians 1:5**, ESV)

Same pattern. The hope is *stored* in heaven. It isn't the same as saying *you* go to heaven.

> Lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither moth nor rust destroys. (**Matthew 6:20**, ESV)

A statement about security and priorities — not a real estate listing. Heaven is the safest vault in the universe.

### Passages That Seem to Point Heavenward — But Read Carefully

> In my Father's house are many rooms. If it were not so, would I have told you that I go to prepare a place for you? And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again and will take you to myself, that where I am you may be also. (**John 14:2-3**, ESV)

This is one of the most beloved "heaven" passages. But look at what Jesus actually says: He goes to prepare a place, and then *He comes back*. "I will come again and will take you to myself." The movement is not us-to-heaven. It is Christ-back-to-us.

> Our citizenship is in heaven, and from it we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ, who will transform our lowly body to be like his glorious body. (**Philippians 3:20-21**, ESV)

Our citizenship is *in* heaven — like having your legal residence in one country while living abroad. But what we're waiting for comes *from* heaven: the Savior who transforms our bodies. The destination isn't heaven. The deliverer comes from there to us.

> They desire a better country, that is, a heavenly one. For God has prepared for them a city. (**Hebrews 11:16**, ESV)

"A heavenly country" — but what city? Hebrews 11:10 says Abraham "was looking forward to the city that has foundations, whose designer and builder is God." That city appears in Revelation 21:2 — and it *comes down out of heaven to earth*.

### The Pattern

Almost every "heaven" verse falls into one of three categories:

1. **Intermediate state** — between death and resurrection (real, conscious, "with Christ" — but temporary and incomplete)
2. **Stored in heaven** — blessings kept safe for future delivery at Christ's return
3. **Heavenly origin** — things that come *from* heaven *to* earth (the Savior, the city, the kingdom)

None of them describe the *final, eternal* destiny of believers as disembodied souls floating in heaven forever.

## III. What the Bible Says About Hell

### The Vocabulary

Scripture uses four distinct words that English translations all render as "hell." They mean very different things.

**Sheol** (Hebrew) — The grave. The realm of the dead. *All* the dead go there, righteous and wicked alike. About 65 Old Testament occurrences. It is not a place of punishment — it is simply death.

> Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might, for there is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in Sheol, to which you are going. (**Ecclesiastes 9:10**, ESV)

> The dead do not praise the LORD, nor do any who go down into silence. (**Psalm 115:17**, ESV)

Sheol is silence, not torment. It is the grave, not a fiery underworld.

**Hades** (Greek) — The New Testament equivalent of Sheol. The realm of the dead. Jesus uses it in the parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:23). At the final judgment, "Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire" (Revelation 20:14) — meaning Hades itself is temporary and is ultimately destroyed.

**Gehenna** (Greek, from Hebrew *Ge-Hinnom*) — The Valley of Hinnom, a real ravine south of Jerusalem. In the Old Testament, it was where children were sacrificed to Molech (2 Kings 23:10; Jeremiah 7:31). King Josiah desecrated it, and by Jesus' day it functioned as Jerusalem's garbage dump — a place where refuse, animal carcasses, and the bodies of executed criminals were burned. Fire was kept burning continuously to consume the waste. Maggots fed on the decay.

This is Jesus' primary word for final punishment. He uses it 11 times. It appears 12 times total in the New Testament. It is not an underground cavern. It is a garbage dump.

**Tartarus** — Used once (2 Peter 2:4), exclusively for fallen angels. Not a human destination.

**Lake of Fire** — Revelation's term (19:20; 20:10, 14-15; 21:8). The final destination of the devil, the beast, the false prophet, and those not in the book of life. Its nature is debated.

### The Key Passages

**Jesus' Gehenna Warnings:**

> Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can *destroy* both soul and body in hell [Gehenna]. (**Matthew 10:28**, ESV)

The word is *destroy* (*apollumi*) — not "torment forever." The same word used for perishing, being ruined, being lost.

> If your hand causes you to sin, cut it off. It is better for you to enter life crippled than with two hands to go to hell [Gehenna], to the unquenchable fire. (**Mark 9:43**, ESV)

"Unquenchable fire" — fire that *cannot be put out*, not fire that never runs out of fuel. The emphasis is that nothing can stop it from completing its work.

> Where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched. (**Mark 9:48**, ESV)

Jesus is quoting Isaiah 66:24. Look up the original:

> They shall go out and look on the dead *bodies* of the men who have rebelled against me. For their worm shall not die, their fire shall not be quenched, and they shall be an abhorrence to all flesh. (**Isaiah 66:24**, ESV)

Isaiah describes *corpses* — dead bodies being consumed by worms and fire. Not living beings writhing in eternal agony. The image is a garbage dump, not a torture chamber.

**The Eternal Punishment Passages:**

> Then he will say to those on his left, "Depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels."... And these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life. (**Matthew 25:41, 46**, ESV)

"Eternal punishment" — but is "eternal" modifying the *act* of punishing (it goes on forever) or the *result* of punishment (it is permanent, irreversible)?

Compare: "Eternal judgment" (Hebrews 6:2) — a judgment with permanent results, not judging forever. "Eternal redemption" (Hebrews 9:12) — a redemption accomplished once with permanent effects, not redeeming forever. "Eternal" (*aionios*) frequently describes the permanence of the outcome, not the duration of the process.

> They will suffer the punishment of eternal *destruction*, away from the presence of the Lord. (**2 Thessalonians 1:9**, ESV)

Destruction. Not torment. The destruction is eternal — permanent, irreversible. But it is *destruction*.

> Sodom and Gomorrah... serve as an example by undergoing a punishment of eternal fire. (**Jude 7**, ESV)

Sodom is not still burning. The fire accomplished its purpose — permanent, irreversible destruction. The *result* of the fire is eternal, not the fire itself.

**The Revelation Passages:**

> The smoke of their torment goes up forever and ever, and they have no rest, day or night. (**Revelation 14:11**, ESV)

This is the strongest verse for eternal conscious torment. But it's in the most heavily symbolic book of the Bible, using imagery drawn from Isaiah 34:10, which describes the judgment of *Edom* — a nation that no longer exists. "Its smoke shall go up forever" was fulfilled in Edom's permanent destruction, not in Edom's eternal burning.

> The devil who had deceived them was thrown into the lake of fire and sulfur where the beast and the false prophet were, and they will be tormented day and night forever and ever. (**Revelation 20:10**, ESV)

This describes the devil, the beast, and the false prophet — supernatural/symbolic entities. Whether human beings share the same fate is the question, not the answer.

> Then Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire. This is the second death, the lake of fire. (**Revelation 20:14**, ESV)

"The second death." Death is *dying*. The second death is the *final* dying. Not eternal living-in-torment.

### The Parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19-31)

This is a parable — Jesus' only story set in the afterlife — and it uses the conventional imagery of His audience (Hades, Abraham's bosom, flames, chasm). It was a well-known Jewish folk tale that Jesus adapted. Its point is not a geography lesson about the afterlife. Its point is in v. 31: "If they do not hear Moses and the Prophets, neither will they be convinced if someone should rise from the dead." The parable is about listening to Scripture *now*, not about the architecture of hell.

### Three Views on Final Punishment

All three are held by Bible-believing Christians. The debate is about interpretation, not whether judgment is real.

**1. Eternal Conscious Torment (Traditional)**
The wicked suffer consciously forever. "Eternal" modifies the experience. Held by Augustine, Calvin, most of church history. Strongest text: Revelation 14:11.

**2. Annihilationism / Conditional Immortality**
The wicked are destroyed — they cease to exist after judgment. "Eternal" describes the permanence of the result, not endless duration. Held by John Stott, Edward Fudge, and a growing number of evangelicals. Strongest texts: Matthew 10:28 ("destroy both soul and body"); 2 Thessalonians 1:9 ("eternal destruction"); 1 Timothy 6:16 (God "alone has immortality" — humans are not inherently immortal).

**3. Eternal Separation (Relational)**
Hell is the experience of existing apart from God — the source of all light, love, goodness, and joy. The suffering is not physical fire but relational emptiness. C.S. Lewis (*The Great Divorce*): "The doors of hell are locked from the inside." This view may complement either of the above rather than replacing them.

## IV. What the Bible Says About Resurrection

If heaven is the waiting room, resurrection is the destination. This is where the Bible's energy is.

### Old Testament

> I know that my Redeemer lives, and at the last he will stand upon the earth. And after my skin has been thus destroyed, yet *in my flesh* I shall see God. (**Job 19:25-26**, ESV)

In my flesh. Not as a disembodied spirit. Job's hope is bodily.

> Your dead shall live; their *bodies* shall rise. You who dwell in the dust, awake and sing for joy! (**Isaiah 26:19**, ESV)

Bodies. Dust. Physical resurrection.

> Many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt. (**Daniel 12:2**, ESV)

"Dust of the earth" — bodies, buried, awakening. Physical.

> I will put my Spirit within you, and you shall live, and I will place you *in your own land*. (**Ezekiel 37:14**, ESV)

The valley of dry bones. Bones reassembled, sinews attached, flesh grows, breath enters. And then — placed *in your own land*. Not in heaven. On the earth.

### Jesus' Teaching

> An hour is coming when all who are in the tombs will hear his voice and come out, those who have done good to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil to the resurrection of judgment. (**John 5:28-29**, ESV)

Out of the tombs. Physical. All humanity.

> This is the will of him who sent me, that I should lose nothing of all that he has given me, but *raise it up* on the last day. (**John 6:39**, ESV)

Raise it up — from death. Not transport to heaven.

> I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live. (**John 11:25**, ESV)

Jesus doesn't say, "I am the ticket to heaven." He says, "I am the *resurrection*."

### Paul's Teaching

> He who raised Christ Jesus from the dead will also give life to your *mortal bodies* through his Spirit who dwells in you. (**Romans 8:11**, ESV)

Mortal bodies given life. Not souls freed from bodies.

> We ourselves, who have the firstfruits of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for adoption as sons, *the redemption of our bodies*. (**Romans 8:23**, ESV)

Redemption *of* our bodies. Not *from* our bodies.

> What is sown is perishable; what is raised is imperishable. It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power. It is sown a natural body; it is raised a *spiritual body*. (**1 Corinthians 15:42-44**, ESV)

"Spiritual body" — *soma pneumatikon*. Not "spirit without body." A body animated and empowered by the Spirit. The contrast is not body vs. no-body. It is natural-body vs. Spirit-powered-body.

> We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be *changed*, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet. For the trumpet will sound, and the dead will be raised imperishable, and we shall be changed. For this perishable body must *put on* the imperishable, and this mortal body must *put on* immortality. (**1 Corinthians 15:51-53**, ESV)

"Put on" — clothing language. The body is upgraded, not discarded. You don't throw away your body; you put new life *over* it.

> The Lord Jesus Christ... will *transform our lowly body* to be like his glorious body. (**Philippians 3:20-21**, ESV)

Transform. Same body. Glorified. Not a replacement — a transformation.

### Jesus' Resurrection Body: The Prototype

The Gospels describe Jesus' risen body as both *physical* and *transformed*:

**Physical:** He could be touched (John 20:27). He ate fish (Luke 24:42-43). He said, "A spirit does not have flesh and bones as you see that I have" (Luke 24:39).

**Transformed:** He appeared through locked doors (John 20:19). He was sometimes unrecognized (Luke 24:16). He ascended bodily (Acts 1:9).

Neither ghost nor resuscitated corpse. Something new — physical yet glorified. And Paul says our resurrection bodies will be like His (Philippians 3:21; 1 John 3:2).

## V. What the Bible Says About the New Earth

If the question is "Where will we spend eternity?" the Bible's answer is not "heaven." It is "a renewed earth, with God."

### The Prophets

> Behold, I create new heavens and a new earth, and the former things shall not be remembered or come into mind... They shall build houses and inhabit them; they shall plant vineyards and eat their fruit. (**Isaiah 65:17, 21**, ESV)

Building houses. Planting vineyards. Eating fruit. Physical activities on a physical earth.

> The wolf shall dwell with the lamb, and the leopard shall lie down with the young goat... They shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain, for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea. (**Isaiah 11:6, 9**, ESV)

*The earth* — transformed, not abandoned.

> The new heavens and the new earth that I make shall remain before me. (**Isaiah 66:22**, ESV)

Enduring. Permanent. The new earth is not a temporary arrangement.

### Paul

> The creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption and obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God. For we know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth until now. (**Romans 8:21-22**, ESV)

Creation *set free*. Not destroyed. Liberated. The earth groans for redemption the way a pregnant woman groans for delivery — the groaning leads to *new life*, not death.

> Through him to reconcile to himself *all things*, whether on earth or in heaven, making peace by the blood of his cross. (**Colossians 1:20**, ESV)

All things reconciled. Earth included, not evacuated.

### Peter

> According to his promise we are waiting for *new heavens and a new earth* in which righteousness dwells. (**2 Peter 3:13**, ESV)

We are waiting for a new *earth*. Righteousness dwells *there* — not in a disembodied heaven.

What about 2 Peter 3:10 — "the heavens will pass away with a roar, and the heavenly bodies will be burned up"? Two options: (1) Total annihilation and replacement. (2) Purification by fire — the way a goldsmith refines metal, burning away impurities but preserving the gold. Jesus' resurrection body is the model: same body, purified, glorified. Creation follows the same pattern.

### Revelation

> Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away... And I saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, *coming down out of heaven from God*, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. (**Revelation 21:1-2**, ESV)

The New Jerusalem comes *down*. Heaven descends to earth. We don't go up — God comes down.

> And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Behold, the *dwelling place of God is with man*. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God." (**Revelation 21:3**, ESV)

This is the climax of the entire Bible. Not humans ascending to God. God descending to humans. Emmanuel — "God with us" — finally, fully, forever.

> He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away. (**Revelation 21:4**, ESV)

No more death. No more crying. On the new earth. With God.

> By its light will the nations walk, and the kings of the earth will bring their glory into it. (**Revelation 21:24**, ESV)

Nations. Kings. Culture. Human civilization isn't erased — it's redeemed and offered to God.

> Then the angel showed me the river of the water of life, bright as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb through the middle of the street of the city; also, on either side of the river, the tree of life with its twelve kinds of fruit... The leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations. (**Revelation 22:1-2**, ESV)

River of life. Tree of life. The bookends of Scripture: what was lost in Genesis 2-3 is restored in Revelation 22. Eden restored — and surpassed.

> They will see his face, and his name will be on their foreheads... And they will reign forever and ever. (**Revelation 22:4-5**, ESV)

Seeing God's face. Reigning. Forever. On the renewed earth.

### One More Verse — Easy to Miss

> You have made them a kingdom and priests to our God, and *they shall reign on the earth*. (**Revelation 5:10**, ESV)

On the earth. Not in heaven. The saints reign on the earth.

## VI. The Rapture Question: Up or Down?

The critical passage is 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17:

> For the Lord himself will descend from heaven with a cry of command, with the voice of an archangel, and with the sound of the trumpet of God. And the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive, who are left, will be *caught up* together with them in the clouds *to meet the Lord* in the air, and so we will always be with the Lord. (**1 Thessalonians 4:16-17**, ESV)

### The Key Word: "To Meet" (*Apantesin*)

"Caught up" is *harpazo* (Latin: *rapturo* — where we get "rapture"). But the purpose clause matters more: caught up *to meet* the Lord.

The Greek word *apantesin* is a technical term. In the ancient world, when a king or emperor approached a city, the citizens would go *out* from the city to **meet** (*apantesin*) him on the road — and then *escort him back into their city*. This was a formal civic custom called the *adventus* (royal arrival). The citizens didn't leave with the king. They went out to welcome him and bring him home with them.

The same word appears in two other New Testament passages:

> At midnight there was a cry, "Here is the bridegroom! Come out *to meet* [*apantesin*] him." (**Matthew 25:6**, ESV)

The virgins go out to meet the bridegroom — and escort him to the wedding feast. They don't leave with him for his house.

> The brothers there... traveled as far as the Forum of Appius and Three Taverns *to meet* [*apantesin*] us. (**Acts 28:15**, ESV)

Roman Christians go out to meet Paul on the road — and escort him back into Rome. They don't leave Rome with Paul.

**The image Paul is painting:** When Christ returns, believers rise to meet Him in the air — as a royal welcoming party — and escort Him back to earth. This is not an evacuation. It is a coronation parade.

### Three Views on the Rapture

**1. Pre-Tribulation Rapture (Dispensationalism)**
The church is secretly removed from earth before a 7-year tribulation. Christ returns invisibly for the church, then visibly 7 years later. Popularized by John Nelson Darby in the 1830s, the Scofield Reference Bible (1909), and the *Left Behind* novels.

*Observation:* No one in church history held this view before the 1830s. It requires two separate "second comings" — one secret and one public — which no single text teaches. And the word *apantesin* works against a departure; it implies a welcome.

**2. Post-Tribulation / Historic Premillennialism**
Believers endure the tribulation and meet Christ as He returns to earth. The "catching up" is the welcoming committee for the returning King. This fits the *apantesin* pattern. It fits Jesus' own words: "Immediately *after* the tribulation of those days... they will see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven" (Matthew 24:29-30, ESV).

**3. Amillennial / General Resurrection**
1 Thessalonians 4 describes the general resurrection at Christ's return. There is no separate "rapture" event distinct from the Second Coming. The church meets Christ and enters the renewed creation together.

### What Jesus Actually Said About Being "Taken"

> Then two men will be in the field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be taken and one left. (**Matthew 24:40-41**, ESV)

This is routinely used to support the rapture. But look at the context — the verses immediately before (v. 37-39):

> As were the days of Noah, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking... and they knew nothing until the flood came and *swept them all away*, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. (**Matthew 24:37-39**, ESV)

In the flood, who was "taken"? The wicked — swept away by judgment. Who was "left"? Noah and his family — the survivors. Being *taken* is the bad outcome. Being *left behind* is the good one.

## VII. The Kingdom: Heaven or Earth?

### Where Does God's Story End?

| What Scripture Describes | Location |
|---|---|
| God dwelling with humanity | **Earth** (Revelation 21:3) |
| New Jerusalem | Comes **down** to earth (Revelation 21:2) |
| Believers reigning | **On the earth** (Revelation 5:10) |
| Tree of life, river of life | **On earth** (Revelation 22:1-2) |
| Nations walking by God's light | **On earth** (Revelation 21:24) |
| Feasting | **On earth** (Isaiah 25:6; Revelation 19:9) |
| Building and planting | **On earth** (Isaiah 65:21-22) |
| Resurrection bodies | **Physical**, for a physical world (1 Corinthians 15) |
| Death abolished | **On earth** (Revelation 21:4) |
| Jesus reigning as King | **On earth** (Zechariah 14:9; Revelation 11:15) |

### The Return of the King

> On that day his feet shall stand on the Mount of Olives that lies before Jerusalem on the east. (**Zechariah 14:4**, ESV)

His feet. On the ground. Physical.

> And the LORD will be king over all the earth. On that day the LORD will be one and his name one. (**Zechariah 14:9**, ESV)

King over the earth. Not taking people away from it.

> This Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will come *in the same way* as you saw him go into heaven. (**Acts 1:11**, ESV)

He left physically from the Mount of Olives. He returns physically to the Mount of Olives. Zechariah 14 and Acts 1 agree — down to the exact location.

> The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever. (**Revelation 11:15**, ESV)

The kingdom of *the world*. Not "the world is abandoned and we go to heaven." The world becomes His kingdom.

## VIII. How Did We Get Here? The Greek Detour

If the Bible so clearly points to resurrection and new earth, how did "dying and going to heaven" become the default Christian belief?

### Plato's Shadow

Greek philosophy — especially Plato (428-348 BC) — taught a sharp division between the material world and the spiritual world:

- **The body** (*soma*) is a prison for the soul. It is material, corrupt, decaying.
- **The soul** (*psyche*) is immortal, divine, longing to escape the body.
- **Death** is liberation — the soul finally escapes its fleshly cage.
- **Salvation** is the soul ascending to the pure, immaterial realm of the Forms.

This framework was already widespread in the Roman Empire when Christianity appeared. When Greek-speaking converts heard the gospel, many instinctively filtered it through Platonic categories:

- Resurrection? Unnecessary — the soul is already immortal.
- New earth? Why? The material world is the problem.
- Bodies in eternity? That's going *backward*.

We see this tension in the New Testament itself:

> When they heard of the resurrection of the dead, some mocked. (**Acts 17:32**, ESV)

The Athenians weren't mocking God. They were mocking the idea that a *body* was worth raising. Why would anyone want to go back into a body?

### The Shift in Church History

**Early church (1st-3rd centuries):** Bodily resurrection and new creation were central. The Apostles' Creed affirms "the resurrection of the body," not "the immortality of the soul." The early fathers pushed back against Gnostic spiritualizing.

**After Constantine (4th century onward):** As Christianity became the Roman state religion, it absorbed more Greek philosophical categories. Augustine (354-430), deeply influenced by Neoplatonism, framed the afterlife primarily as the soul's vision of God (*beatific vision*). The body and earth became secondary.

**Medieval period:** Heaven and hell as destinations for the soul became the dominant framework. Resurrection was still affirmed in the creeds, but it lost its central place. The popular imagination shifted from "resurrection on a new earth" to "my soul going to heaven."

**Reformation:** The Reformers recovered many doctrines but largely kept the medieval heaven-and-hell framework intact.

**Modern era:** The *Left Behind* novels (1995-2007) cemented the rapture-to-heaven narrative in popular American Christianity. For many people, *Left Behind* is more formative than the Bible on end times.

### The Hebrew Original

The Hebrew worldview that produced the Bible sees reality differently:

- **The body** is not a prison — it is God's good creation. "God formed the man of dust from the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life" (Genesis 2:7). The body is where God's breath lives.
- **The soul** is not a separate entity trapped inside — it *is* the whole living person. *Nephesh* (Hebrew for "soul") means "living being," not "ghost inside a body."
- **Death** is not liberation — it is the enemy. "The last enemy to be destroyed is death" (1 Corinthians 15:26). You don't celebrate an enemy.
- **Salvation** is not escape from creation — it is creation's redemption. "The creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption" (Romans 8:21).

The hope is not the soul escaping the body to float in an immaterial heaven. The hope is resurrection — new bodies, new earth, God with us.

## IX. Putting It All Together

### The Biblical Story in Five Acts

**Act 1 — Eden:** God creates a good physical world. He walks with humanity in the garden. Heaven and earth overlap. God dwells with His people.

**Act 2 — The Fall:** Sin enters. Death reigns. Heaven and earth are torn apart. Humanity is exiled from God's presence.

**Act 3 — Israel:** God begins the rescue operation. He chooses a people, gives them a land, dwells among them in the tabernacle and temple. Partial overlap of heaven and earth — but incomplete.

**Act 4 — Jesus:** God Himself enters creation. "The Word became flesh and *tabernacled* among us" (John 1:14 — *eskenosen*, literally "pitched His tent"). In Jesus, heaven and earth overlap perfectly in one person. He dies, rises bodily, and ascends — taking resurrected humanity into God's space.

**Act 5 — New Creation:** Heaven comes *down*. God makes His dwelling with humanity. The New Jerusalem descends. Heaven and earth are fully reunited. The story doesn't end with us going *up* to God. It ends with God coming *down* to us.

### What Happens When You Die?

**Immediately:** You are "with Christ" (Philippians 1:23; Luke 23:43). This is real, conscious, and far better than this life. But it is *intermediate* — incomplete. You are "unclothed" (2 Corinthians 5:4), waiting for your resurrection body.

**At Christ's return:** The dead are raised. The living are transformed. All believers receive resurrection bodies — physical, imperishable, glorious, Spirit-powered. This is the hope Paul yearns for (Romans 8:23).

**Forever:** Embodied life on the renewed earth, with God dwelling among us. Not floating on clouds. Not strumming harps. Building, planting, feasting, worshiping, reigning — with resurrection bodies in a resurrection world, and the face of God shining over all of it.

### The Diagram

**Popular View:**

Life → Death → Soul goes to Heaven (forever) ↗ or Soul goes to Hell (forever) ↘

**Biblical View:**

Life → Death → Intermediate State (with Christ, but incomplete) → Christ Returns → Resurrection (new body) → New Earth (God dwells with us forever)

### The Bottom Line

The question is not "Will you go to heaven when you die?" The question is "Will you be raised when Christ returns?"

Heaven is real. Being "with Christ" after death is real. But heaven is not the destination — it is the *waiting room*.

The destination is a renewed earth, with a resurrection body, with God dwelling in our midst. That is what the prophets foretold, what Jesus inaugurated, what the apostles proclaimed, and what Revelation describes in its final chapters.

Not escape from creation. Its redemption.

Not souls ascending to heaven. God descending to earth.

Not "all new things." "All things made *new*."

> Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. (**Revelation 21:3**, ESV)
